# Lean Research: When You Have No Budget, No UXR, and No Time

## The Real Situation in Most Product Companies

Most junior designers don't work in a company with a dedicated UX research team. They're the only designer, or one of two. The PM says "we need to ship this in 3 weeks." There's no time for a 3-month research phase.

This is normal. This is most of the industry.

The skill you need is knowing how to get *enough* research, fast — not perfect research.

---

## The 5 Lean Research Methods You'll Actually Use

### 1. Guerrilla Testing (5-Minute User Tests)
**What it is:** You grab anyone — a colleague, a family member, someone at a café — and ask them to do a specific task in the product while you watch.

**How to do it:**
- Pick ONE task to test ("Try to add this item to your cart and check out")
- Say: "I want to see how this works for you. There are no wrong answers. Think out loud."
- Shut up and watch. Don't help them.
- Note where they hesitate, click the wrong thing, or look confused.

**How many:** 5 people reveals ~85% of usability issues. You don't need 50.

**When to use:** Before finalizing any new flow or major UI change.

### 2. 5-Second Test
**What it is:** Show someone a screen for 5 seconds, then ask: "What do you remember? What's this page about? What would you do next?"

**Why it works:** If someone can't tell you the purpose of your screen in 5 seconds, your hierarchy is wrong.

**Tools:** Maze, UsabilityHub (free tiers exist), or literally just showing someone on your phone.

### 3. Reading Analytics as Research
**What it is:** Using product data to infer user behavior.

**What to look for:**
- Where do users drop off in the funnel?
- What pages have high bounce rates?
- What features have low usage despite being prominent?
- What do search queries inside the product tell you about what users can't find?

**This is underrated.** Analytics is research. A 72% drop-off at step 3 of onboarding is research. Treat it like that.

### 4. Stakeholder Interviews as Research
**What it is:** Talking to the PM, sales team, customer support, and engineers to extract user knowledge they already have.

**The support team is a goldmine.** They hear user complaints all day. Ask them: "What are the top 5 things users complain about or ask for help with?" That's user research you can do in 30 minutes.

**Questions to ask stakeholders:**
- "Who is this product for, specifically? Describe your most common user."
- "What do users love most? What do they hate most?"
- "What's the most common support ticket or complaint?"
- "What would you fix first if you could?"

### 5. Heatmaps & Session Recordings
**Tools:** Hotjar (free tier), Microsoft Clarity (free), FullStory.

**Heatmaps tell you:**
- Where users click (click heatmaps)
- How far they scroll (scroll heatmaps)
- What they ignore vs. what draws attention

**Session recordings tell you:**
- The exact path a real user took
- Where they got stuck or rage-clicked
- What they tried before giving up

**How to use:** Watch 10-15 session recordings of users on a specific flow. You'll see patterns within that many.

## How to Present Lean Research to a PM or Client

Don't apologize for not having a 200-person study. Frame it right:

**Wrong:** "We didn't have time for proper research, so we just tested with 5 people"

**Right:** "I ran quick usability tests with 5 representative users and identified 3 critical issues. Research shows 5 users reveal 80%+ of usability problems, so this gives us high-confidence direction to proceed."

---

## Research Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Leading questions:** "Did you find that button easy to use?" → Don't do this. Ask "what would you do next on this screen?"
- **Testing with designers:** They know too much. Test with actual target users or people unfamiliar with the product.
- **Over-researching to avoid designing:** Research is not an excuse to delay decisions. Know when you have enough.
- **Presenting research without synthesis:** "Here are 40 quotes from users" is not a research deliverable. "Here are 3 themes that emerged, with supporting evidence" is.
