# 30-Day UX Mastery Roadmap
### Target: 25 LPA+ UX / Product Designer Roles
**10 hours/day | 300 total hours | 4 phases**

---

## How This Plan Works

Every day has four parts:

1. **The Big Idea** — one core concept to absorb (not memorize — understand)
2. **Study Session** — what to read or re-read from the source material (2 hours)
3. **Practice Session** — a focused activity to apply the concept immediately (3 hours)
4. **Daily Assignment** — labeled Short / Medium / High, this is your main output for the day (3-4 hours)
5. **Reflection** — 30 minutes to write what clicked and what confused you

The remaining 30-60 minutes each day is buffer for review, re-reading, or sleep (yes, 10 hours of deep cognitive work is exhausting — don't skip sleep).

---

## The Priority Framework

This plan is built around one truth: **AI will automate the execution of UX work. It cannot automate the thinking behind it.**

The skills you will invest the most time in are the ones that require a human mind, human empathy, and human judgment. Everything else — documentation, rationale writing, pattern research — you will learn to do WITH AI as your assistant.

**Tier 1 — Human-only skills (highest priority, deepest practice):**
- Reading the emotion behind user behavior, not just the click data
- Knowing which user pain is real vs. stated
- Contextual judgment in ambiguous, messy, real-world situations
- Finding the root cause of a problem, not just the surface symptom
- Making confident decisions when data is incomplete
- Building trust in real conversations with stakeholders and users

**Tier 2 — Business-critical skills (second priority):**
- Speaking the language of metrics (CR, AOV, Retention, DAU)
- Translating any business input into a user-centered design direction
- Planning projects of any scale — from 1-day sprint to 3-month roadmap
- Presenting and defending design decisions to stakeholders

**Tier 3 — Craft + packaging skills (third priority):**
- UX audit execution
- Case study writing (all 5 formats)
- Portfolio scan-worthiness
- Verbal case study preparation

# WEEK 1: HUMAN-FIRST FOUNDATION
## Days 1-7

**The Big Goal This Week:** Build the mental model of what UX actually is. Not the textbook version. The version that makes a 25 LPA company sit up when you walk into a room.

Most designers at your level already know the words. They say "user journey" and "affordance" and "persona." But they use them like decorations — not as real tools for thinking. This week, you will make these words mean something again.

By Day 7, you should be able to look at any product, any screen, and immediately start thinking in terms of: what is the user trying to accomplish, what emotions are they having right now, and what does the business need from this interaction? Without prompts. Without frameworks. Just as a natural reflex.

---

### DAY 1: What UX Actually Is

**The Big Idea:** UX is not about screens. It is about the emotions a person has while moving through a journey toward a goal. Your job is to design those emotions — not just the buttons that enable them.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read these two transcripts carefully:
- "Give me 14 minutes — I'll change the way you understand UX Design" (the Manu concert story)
- "What a UX Designer Actually Does — Not What You Think" (the Uber booking flow)

While reading, actively note every time Anudeep connects a screen element to a human emotion. The search bar on Uber is not just a box — it is an affordance that triggers a "tap" behavior because of the signifier it communicates, and gives feedback to confirm it worked. These three words — affordance, signifier, feedback — are the actual architecture of experience design. They are not jargon. They are your operating vocabulary.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Open Swiggy on your phone. Order something (or simulate). Write the full journey as a story — exactly like the Manu concert story. Include:
- Manu's goal (what the user wants to achieve)
- The starting point (where does the journey begin)
- The ending point (where does it end)
- Every decision the user makes along the way
- Every emotion at each stage — both positive and negative
- Every product element they interact with (search bar, restaurant card, checkout button, delivery tracking)

Do NOT write this as a list of screens. Write it as a story. First person if it helps. "I open Swiggy and immediately see the search bar at the top. I feel slightly overwhelmed by the restaurant cards below it — there are 40 options and I don't know where to look first..." That kind of writing.

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Take the Razorpay payment checkout flow (or any payment flow you've used). Write a 400-word story of the user's journey through it. Include at minimum 6 distinct emotions across the flow. At the end, write one sentence: "The moment where this experience most needs improvement is ___ because the user feels ___."

**Reflection:**
Write down: what surprised you about thinking in emotions instead of screens? Where did you struggle to identify emotions? That struggle is data about where your empathy muscle is weak.

**✅ Right direction:** You are building a reflex to always start with the human before the screen.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Writing the journey as a numbered list of actions with no emotional layer. That is utilitarian documentation, not UX thinking. A UX designer who cannot name emotions is a glorified screen-layout person.

### DAY 2: The UX Designer's Mental Model — Ambiguity to Clarity

**The Big Idea:** Your job is not to execute briefs. Your job is to turn ambiguity into clarity. Every input you receive — a PM's vague request, a screenshot, a PRD, a verbal complaint — is just ambiguity at different stages. Your value is in bringing clarity to it.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read these two transcripts:
- "How does a UX Designer work — Model Explained"
- "How to Translate Business Requirements to UX Design"

Pay specific attention to the part where Anudeep talks about the value creation triangle: User / Business / Designer. Your job is not to be a craftsperson between the PM and the developer. Your job is to be the person who makes the user and business connection work through experience design.

Also note: Anudeep says the most important question you can ask a PM is "What business metrics is this trying to move?" This question changes the entire nature of the conversation. Memorize this question. Practice it until it comes naturally.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take 5 different types of "ambiguous input" you might receive in your next job. Write what each one looks like and what your first step would be for each:

1. PM shows you a competitor's feature on their phone and says "can we make something like this?"
2. PM sends you a 12-page PRD document over Slack
3. PM verbally says in a standup "users are complaining about the onboarding"
4. PM says "we have a board meeting in 3 days and need to show something for the new dashboard"
5. PM places a hand-drawn sketch on your desk and says "I was thinking something like this"

For each: What is the ambiguity? What is the first question you ask? What is the first thing you do?

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Your PM sends you this message over Slack: "Hey, we need to add a notification bell to the dashboard. Can you start on designs?"

Write your full response — not the designs, but what you say back. Use business metric language. Write the 3-4 clarifying questions you would ask before touching Figma. Then write what the wrong response looks like (a junior designer who just opens Figma immediately).

**Reflection:**
The gap between a 10 LPA and a 25 LPA designer is often just this: the 25 LPA designer asks one more question before starting. They ask "what are we trying to achieve?" not "what should it look like?" Write about where you personally tend to skip this step.

**✅ Right direction:** Treating every brief as a starting point for a conversation, not a starting point for Figma.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Opening Figma immediately when you get a brief. This is called "solutionizing" — jumping to solution before understanding the problem. It is the most common junior mistake and it produces work that gets rejected in review.

### DAY 3: Business Metrics — The Language That Gets You Paid

**The Big Idea:** A designer who can speak in metrics is worth double. Not because metrics are everything — but because business people think in metrics, and if you speak their language, they trust you more, give you more autonomy, and eventually pay you more.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read these sources carefully:
- "4 KPIs Every UX Designer Must Learn" transcript
- "Harsh Truth About Business Every UI/UX Designer Must Learn" transcript
- The metrics-primer.md reference (the full document)

Do not just read for information. Read to understand WHY each metric exists. Conversion Rate exists because businesses need to know if people are actually doing the thing they want them to do. Retention Rate exists because getting a new customer costs 5x more than keeping an existing one. Daily Active Users exists because time spent in your product = attention = ad revenue or engagement data. Understanding the WHY behind each metric is what separates someone who can recite definitions from someone who can actually use them in design decisions.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Go through your three most significant past projects (ESOP grant flow, Market Benchmarking tool, Manager View). For each one, answer these questions in writing:

- What was the product's primary business model? How did it make money or save money?
- Which business metric was your project most directly trying to move?
- Did the experience you designed actually help move that metric? How? If you don't know, what data would you have needed to find out?
- If you had to add one metric you would track to evaluate the success of your design, what would it be?

This is not a hypothetical exercise. This is the actual question senior interviewers will ask you. Practice answering it about your real work.

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Take the Swiggy or Zomato food ordering flow. Write three short paragraphs (one per metric):

1. How does the current design of the restaurant listing page impact conversion rate? What is one specific change you would make to improve it, and why would that change move the metric?
2. How does the current "frequently ordered together" or "add-on" section impact average order value? Is it working? What would make it work better?
3. How does the post-order experience (tracking, rating, re-order) impact retention? What one addition would most improve repeat ordering?

Each paragraph should end with: "The expected metric impact is ___ because ___."

**Reflection:**
Write down: which metric do you feel most comfortable explaining? Which one still feels fuzzy? The fuzzy ones are the ones you need to practice speaking out loud — not just writing.

**✅ Right direction:** Framing design decisions in terms of "this will improve [metric] by doing [specific thing] because [reason]."
**❌ Wrong direction:** Saying "this will improve the user experience." That sentence means nothing in a business meeting. It is a filler phrase. The moment you say it, a PM or senior stakeholder internally discounts your point. Replace it with the specific metric and the specific mechanism.

### DAY 4: Emotion Mapping — The Skill AI Will Never Have

**The Big Idea:** Every screen has two types of opportunities — gaps (places where negative emotions are happening that you can fix) and opportunities (places where positive emotions are happening that you can amplify). Most junior designers only look for gaps. Senior designers hunt both.

This is also the skill most directly dependent on you being human. AI can tell you where users dropped off. It cannot tell you what they felt at that moment. You can — if you train yourself to think this way.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read this section carefully:
- "How to Translate Business Requirements to UX Design" transcript — specifically the Myntra emotion mapping classroom session

Notice how Anudeep walks through the Myntra product listing page with his students, mapping emotions in real-time. He is not using a tool or a template. He is asking simple human questions: "What is the user thinking here? What emotion are they feeling? Is that a gap or an opportunity?" Practice this way of seeing.

Also read from the UX Sensei skill: "The Human Skills AI Cannot Replicate" — specifically points 1, 2, and 7 about reading emotion, understanding real vs. stated pain, and the 1-minute user insight.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take the LinkedIn job search flow (or the Naukri job search flow — you've likely used both). Map the full emotion journey across these stages:
- Opening the app with intent to job hunt
- Browsing job listings
- Reading one specific job description
- Clicking "Easy Apply"
- Filling the application form
- Submitting and waiting for confirmation

For each stage, write:
- What is the user thinking? (specific thoughts, not generic)
- What emotion are they feeling? (name it precisely — not "frustrated" but "anxious about whether their application was received correctly")
- Is this a gap (negative emotion to fix) or an opportunity (positive emotion to amplify)?
- What design change would address this?

You should generate at least 10 mapped moments across the full flow.

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
Take two different users with two different contexts using the same flow:

User A: A fresh graduate using LinkedIn to apply for their first job, feeling nervous and unsure.
User B: An experienced designer with 4 years of experience, applying casually while employed, feeling confident but impatient.

Map the emotion journey for BOTH users across the LinkedIn job application flow. Write where their emotions are the same, and where they diverge. Write one design decision that helps both users, and one design decision that primarily helps User A but might not matter to User B.

This is a senior-level exercise. It trains you to hold multiple user perspectives simultaneously — something no AI tool can do from lived experience.

**Reflection:**
Where did you make assumptions about user emotions instead of actually reasoning about them? Write 3 emotions you initially wrote and then changed when you thought harder. This shows you where your default assumptions live.

**✅ Right direction:** Asking "what is the user FEELING at this moment?" as a design question, not as a soft add-on.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Only mapping pain points and calling them "friction." This produces designs that fix problems but do not create joy. A great product does both. Think about why you love using Figma even on a hard day — it is not because it has no friction. It is because it creates moments of genuine delight.

### DAY 5: Business Metrics in Practice — Presenting Decisions Like a Senior

**The Big Idea:** Knowing what a metric is and being able to talk about it confidently in a room are two completely different things. Today you build the bridge between theoretical knowledge and practical fluency.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read these carefully:
- The stakeholder-communication.md reference (full document)
- "Screens Aren't Enough in a UX Portfolio" transcript
- The relevant section of "How does a UX Designer work" about the KPI conversation

Focus specifically on the design rationale formula:
**"I chose [X] because [user need/pain point], which is supported by [evidence/principle]. The alternative was [Y], but it has [trade-off]. The expected outcome is [Z]."**

This formula is your sword. Every time someone asks "why did you design it this way?" — you use this formula. It takes 30 seconds to say. It communicates three things simultaneously: you understand the user, you considered alternatives, and you can connect your work to outcomes. That is exactly what a 25 LPA company needs to hear.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Go through your ESOP grant management project (or your most complex HR tech project). Pick 5 specific design decisions you made. For each one, write the full design rationale formula.

If you don't remember the exact decisions, that's okay — reconstruct them. Think about: why did you use a table view instead of a card view? Why did you put the status indicator in that specific position? Why did you use a multi-step form instead of a single-page form? These are real decisions you made. Write the rationale.

If any of the 5 rationales comes out as "because it looked better" or "because that's what the PM wanted" — that is a gap. Rewrite it using evidence (even if you have to look up a UX principle to support it retroactively).

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Simulate a design review. A senior PM at your new company asks you three questions about one of your past projects:

1. "Why did you choose [a specific layout or component] for this feature?"
2. "We thought about doing [alternative approach]. Why didn't you go that way?"
3. "How will we know if this design is working? What should we measure?"

Write your answers to all three questions. Each answer should use the design rationale formula. Each answer should include the metric that the decision affects.

**Reflection:**
Which of the 5 rationales was hardest to write? That decision is the one you made on instinct rather than evidence. This is not bad — senior designers make instinct-based decisions too. But the skill is being able to reconstruct the reasoning behind instinct and articulate it clearly.

**✅ Right direction:** Presenting designs by starting with the user problem and the business goal, then explaining your key decisions, and ending with how you'd measure success.
**❌ Wrong direction:** The "tour guide" presentation: "So here's the homepage, and below that I put the features section, and then there's a CTA at the bottom..." This describes what they can already see. It contributes nothing. It signals that you think your job is to explain screens, not defend decisions.

### DAY 6: Project Scale Planning — The Framework You've Been Missing

**The Big Idea:** There is no one "correct" UX process. The right process depends entirely on the scale of the work, the constraints of the business, and the amount of time you have. A senior designer can deliver great work in 1 day. The same designer can also plan a 2.5-month project roadmap. The difference is knowing how to apply divergent and convergent thinking at the right moments.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read these carefully:
- "Harsh Truth About Business Every UI/UX Designer Must Learn" transcript (the divergent/convergent section + constraints section)
- "How to understand UX Process — Harsh Reality" transcript (the non-linear process section)

The key insight: UX process is not a procedure you follow. It is a practice you develop. The double diamond is not a checklist — it is two modes of thinking (divergent: generate options, convergent: make decisions) that you apply at whatever scale and in whatever order the situation demands.

**The Project Scale Framework — Study This Carefully:**

**Scale 1: 1-Day Feature Sprint**
When a PM asks you to design something by end of day.

Your 10-hour allocation:
- 1 hour: Understand the business goal — one question: "What metric are we trying to move?"
- 1 hour: Layer 1 audit using heuristic lenses — identify what's broken in under 10 minutes
- 1.5 hours: Divergent sketching — 3 completely different rough approaches
- 30 min: Convergent selection — pick 1 and write the rationale in 3 sentences
- 4 hours: High-fidelity execution in Figma
- 1 hour: Write the decision story and prepare verbal walkthrough
- 1 hour: Buffer / stakeholder alignment

What you SKIP at this scale: User interviews, extensive research, multiple rounds of iteration. You rely on your heuristic knowledge and your empathy as a designer. You are being paid to make fast, informed decisions under pressure. That is a skill.

**Scale 2: 1-Week Feature Sprint**
When you have Monday to Friday for one feature.

Day-by-day:
- Day 1: Scope definition + 3-person guerrilla test + heuristic audit (2 hours total for both)
- Day 2: Root cause analysis + divergent ideation (10 ideas minimum)
- Day 3: Convergent selection + low-fidelity wireframes (rough, not polished)
- Day 4: High-fidelity design + edge cases + error states
- Day 5: Decision documentation + stakeholder presentation prep + verbal walkthrough

What you SKIP: Formal user research, multiple iteration rounds. What you ADD vs. 1-day: Real user input (guerrilla test), more ideation time, proper edge case thinking.

**Scale 3: 1-Month Project**
When you have meaningful time to do this well.

Week 1: Discovery — user interviews (5 people), analytics audit, stakeholder interviews, competitive analysis
Week 2: Problem definition + full ideation sprint + architecture mapping (story-first, never sitemaps)
Week 3: Iterative design — low fidelity to high fidelity, testing with 3-5 users mid-cycle
Week 4: Final design + documentation + presentation preparation

**Scale 4: 2.5-Month Project**
When you are designing something significant that will ship at scale.

Month 1: Deep research sprint — quantitative (analytics) + qualitative (interviews, session recordings) + competitive analysis + stakeholder alignment
Month 2: Design sprints — divergent exploration, testing, iteration, cross-functional collaboration
Last 2 weeks: Polish + documentation + handoff + measurement planning

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take this brief: "We want to add bulk email notifications to the ESOP grant management platform. Employees should be able to receive updates when their grant status changes."

Map out your plan for each of the 4 scales:
- 1 day: What do you prioritize? What do you skip?
- 1 week: What does each day look like?
- 1 month: What does each week look like?
- 2.5 months: What does each month look like?

Notice where your process changes and more importantly WHY it changes. Write down: at what scale do you have time to talk to real users? At what scale can you afford to explore 10 ideas? At what scale must you make a decision on Day 1 and iterate later?

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
Monday morning. Your PM walks over to you and says: "Hey, our users are complaining that it takes too long to find a specific grant in the management table. Can you fix this by Wednesday?"

Today is Monday. You have until Wednesday end of day. That is a 2-day sprint.

Write your full plan:
- What is the first question you ask the PM?
- What do you do in the first 2 hours of Monday?
- What do you build on Monday?
- What do you validate or test on Tuesday?
- What do you deliver by Wednesday EOD?
- What rationale do you write for your design decision?
- How do you present this in a 5-minute Wednesday review?

This is a real situation you will face. Your answer is a living plan you can refer back to.

**Reflection:**
Where do you default to spending too much time? Most junior designers either over-research (delaying decisions) or under-research (making assumptions). Which pattern is yours? Write it down honestly, because knowing your own bias is the first step to correcting it.

**✅ Right direction:** Calibrating your process to the scale of the work. Delivering great work in 1 day when that's what's needed. Expanding the process when time allows.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Applying the same "full UX process" to every task regardless of scale. When a PM gives you 1 day and you come back saying "I need 3 weeks to do this properly," you are not being thorough — you are being inflexible. This is a career-limiting pattern at fast-moving product companies.

### DAY 7: Week 1 Integration Day

**The Big Idea:** Everything you have learned this week connects. Journey thinking connects to emotion mapping. Emotion mapping connects to business metrics (because emotions drive conversions). Business metrics connect to design decisions. Design decisions need rationale. Rationale needs the scale framework to know how much evidence is required.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
No new material today. Re-read your daily reflections from Days 1-6. Look for patterns:
- Where did you struggle most?
- What concept clicked fastest?
- What question keeps coming up that you haven't answered yet?

Spend 1 hour re-reading the design rationale formula examples and the scale planning framework from Days 5 and 6. These are the two tools you will use most immediately in your next job.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Live audit practice. Open one of your existing HR tech products or a competitor product (Darwinbox, Keka, or Rippling). Spend 3 hours just using it as a user — ordering things, navigating flows, completing tasks. Write continuously as you go. Every time you feel anything — confusion, delight, frustration, surprise — write it down immediately.

At the end, you should have a messy, unformatted document of raw observations. This is what real audit thinking looks like before it is organized.

**Daily Assignment — HIGH:**
Take your ESOP grant management project. Rewrite the opening section of its case study using ONLY what you have learned this week.

The new opening must include:
- The user journey (who is the user, what are they trying to accomplish, where does their journey start and end)
- 3 specific emotions from that journey — one positive, one negative, one that is both (ambivalent)
- The business metric this project was trying to move
- Your core design decision and its rationale using the formula from Day 5
- How you would measure whether the design worked

There is no template for this. You are writing it from the inside out — from the human experience, to the business impact, to the design decision. This is the opposite of how most case studies are written (which go from "I did research" to "here are my wireframes" to "here is the final design").

The output does not need to be polished. It needs to be honest and human-first.

# WEEK 2: PROBLEM FINDING AND THE AUDIT
## Days 8-14

**The Big Goal This Week:** Build the ability to look at any product and immediately see what is broken, why it is broken, and what the real problem is (not the surface symptom). This is the skill that separates designers who ship features from designers who solve problems.

The biggest mistake you will unlearn this week is the tendency to treat the first problem you see as the real problem. It almost never is. The surface problem is what users say. The root problem is why they are saying it. Your job is to find the root.

---

### DAY 8: The 3-Layer UX Audit

**The Big Idea:** A UX audit is not a checklist. It is a structured way of seeing what your eyes would naturally miss. The 3-layer framework gives you a repeatable approach: gut scan, heuristic pass, data pass. Each layer reveals a different class of problem.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read the full audit-and-cro.md reference document carefully. While reading, mark every insight that connects to business impact — specifically the places where it says "high drop-off at this step = UX problem" or "high bounce usually means the page isn't communicating value fast enough." These connections between UX findings and business impact are exactly what your stakeholders care about.

Also re-read the Anudeep IRCTC case study review — specifically his feedback on the business goal section, where he explains why "increasing conversions" was the wrong goal for IRCTC and "increasing DAU" was the correct one. This is Layer 3 thinking: connecting the audit finding to the right business metric.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Do a full 3-layer audit on one product you use daily. Choose something you genuinely use — not something you think would be impressive.

Layer 1 (30 minutes): Use the product as a first-time user. Write your raw reactions. Ask: What is this screen trying to get me to do? Is it obvious? What is the first thing my eye goes to? What would make me leave right now?

Layer 2 (60 minutes): Go screen by screen through one core flow. Check against the 5 core heuristics:
1. Clarity of purpose — does every screen have one clear action?
2. Feedback and system status — does the product confirm that things are working?
3. Error prevention and recovery — can users make mistakes? Can they recover easily?
4. Consistency — do similar things look and behave the same way?
5. Cognitive load — how much is the user being asked to process at once?

For each issue found: What is it? Where is it? Why does it matter for the user? Severity (H/M/L)?

Layer 3 (30 minutes): If you don't have real analytics (you probably don't), state what data you'd want to validate each of your Layer 2 findings. "To validate this, I'd look at ___." This shows you understand the data layer even when you don't have access to it.

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Write a 5-finding audit summary in the format a senior designer would share with a PM:

For each finding, write:
- What is the problem (specific, not generic)
- Where it occurs in the flow (screen name or step number)
- Why it likely matters for the business (which metric it affects)
- One specific recommendation to fix it
- Priority: High / Medium / Low (with your reasoning)

The format should be readable in under 5 minutes. No fluff. No academic language. Just the 5 most important findings and what to do about them.

**Reflection:**
Which layer was hardest? Most designers find Layer 1 (gut scan) harder than expected — not because it requires knowledge but because they overthink it. The gut scan is supposed to be messy and immediate. If you were writing carefully and slowly during Layer 1, you were in your head, not in the user's shoes.

**✅ Right direction:** An audit that connects every finding to a business metric and ends with prioritized, actionable recommendations.
**❌ Wrong direction:** An audit that lists 20 problems without prioritizing them or connecting them to impact. This is called "a document nobody reads." Senior designers learn that a 5-finding audit with clear recommendations is worth 10 times more than a 50-finding audit with no clear action.

### DAY 9: Root Cause Thinking — The Senior Designer's Superpower

**The Big Idea:** Every design problem has a surface symptom and a root cause. Surface: "the button is hard to find." Root: "users don't expect to find this feature in this part of their journey." If you fix the surface symptom without addressing the root cause, the problem will come back in a different form.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read the hot seat reviews carefully — specifically these moments:
- Anudeep's feedback to Isha about "going to the root level" (Daily Objects AOV project)
- Anudeep's feedback to Salony about understanding the REAL journey vs. the assumed one (Nykaa quiz flow)
- The IRCTC review, where he explains that the root problem was not "bad UI" but "incomplete AI interaction layer"

In all three cases, the student started designing at the surface level. Anudeep pushed them deeper by asking: why does this problem exist? What behavior is actually happening? What is the user actually trying to do at this point?

Also read: "How to Translate Business Requirements to UX Design" — the section where he talks about pausing to check whether you are solving the root problem or a surface symptom.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take 3 design problems you have personally faced in your HR tech work (ESOP, benchmarking, or any other project). For each one, do a "5 Whys" analysis:

State the surface problem. Ask "why does this problem exist?" Get an answer. Ask "why does THAT exist?" Get an answer. Ask "why" two more times. The 4th or 5th "why" is usually close to the root cause.

Write this out in full for all 3 problems. Then write: was the solution you shipped addressing the surface or the root? If surface — what would a root-level solution have looked like?

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
A PM sends you this feedback: "Our users are not using the bulk actions feature in the grant management table. They're doing everything one by one."

Write your root cause analysis:
1. What is the surface observation?
2. Ask "why" 3 times. What are the possible root causes?
3. Which root cause would you prioritize investigating first and why?
4. What would you design differently if the root cause is "users don't know it exists" vs. "users don't trust it will work correctly" vs. "users are worried about making a mistake at scale"?

Same feature. Three different root causes. Three completely different design directions. This is why root cause matters.

**Reflection:**
Think about a decision you made in a past project that you later realized was fixing the wrong problem. Write about it honestly. What did you miss? What would you do differently? This reflection is worth more than any textbook exercise.

**✅ Right direction:** Always asking "but WHY is this a problem?" at least 3 times before deciding what to design.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Redesigning something because "it looks old" or "the PM said so" without understanding why it is underperforming. This leads to solutions that are technically complete but experientially meaningless.

### DAY 10: Idea Generation — Emotion-First Thinking

**The Big Idea:** The best ideas in UX do not come from brainstorming sessions. They come from a deep understanding of what the user is feeling at a specific moment. When you map an emotion correctly, the solution suggests itself.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Re-read the Myntra classroom session from "How to Translate Business Requirements to UX Design" — specifically the moment where Anudeep walks through average order value and shows how understanding the emotion "I wonder if there's something that goes with this t-shirt" directly generates the idea "show outfit combinations." He doesn't brainstorm an idea out of thin air. He reads the emotion and the idea flows from it.

Also read the Ola hot seat review — specifically where Anudeep explains the Samsung visit retention idea. He starts from "what is the user thinking at this moment?" and arrives at a product feature that the student had not conceived. Pure emotion-first ideation.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take the LinkedIn connection/messaging flow. Map 6 distinct user emotions across the flow. For each emotion, generate 2 ideas that directly respond to that emotion — not general "improvements" but specific responses to the specific feeling.

So if the emotion is "I feel awkward sending a connection request to someone I don't know well," the ideas might be:
1. Show 3 mutual connections and one shared interest as context before sending
2. Offer a "soft connection" mode where both parties receive a notification but neither has to explicitly accept/reject

These ideas emerged from the emotion, not from generic feature thinking.

After doing this for all 6 emotions, you should have 12 ideas. Review them. Which 3 are most likely to move a business metric? Which 3 are most valuable to the user? Write your prioritized top 2.

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
Take one of the following problem statements (pick the one most interesting to you):

Option A: "We want to improve the onboarding experience of Keka (or any HR SaaS) for new employees on their first day."

Option B: "We want to reduce the number of support tickets raised by managers in the ESOP platform."

Option C: "We want to increase the frequency of performance review submissions in a 360 feedback tool."

Do the following:
1. Identify the user journey for this scenario
2. Map 6 emotions across the journey
3. Generate 2 ideas per emotion (12 total)
4. Converge to your top 3 ideas
5. For each top idea, write: what emotion does it address, what is the business metric it affects, and what would you need to validate it?

**Reflection:**
Notice which emotion was hardest to generate ideas for. Usually it is the ambivalent emotions — the ones that are neither strongly positive nor strongly negative. These are the richest opportunities in any product. Users in ambivalent states are the most receptive to a well-designed nudge.

**✅ Right direction:** Asking "what is the user feeling at this moment?" before asking "what should the design do?"
**❌ Wrong direction:** Running a "brainstorming session" that generates random feature ideas without anchoring them to a specific user emotion in a specific moment. Random ideas produce random outcomes.

### DAY 11: Divergent and Convergent Thinking in Practice

**The Big Idea:** Divergent thinking = generate as many options as possible, ignoring all constraints. Convergent thinking = select the best option within real constraints. Senior designers can switch between these modes on demand. Most juniors stay in one mode and wonder why their work either feels uncreative or impossible to ship.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read these:
- "Harsh Truth About Business" transcript (double diamond section)
- The relevant section of "How to Translate Business Requirements" on designing for constraints

Note especially: divergent thinking is about removing constraints temporarily so your imagination can operate freely. Convergent thinking is about adding constraints back so your decisions become executable. The quality of your final design depends on how freely you can diverge and how clearly you can converge.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take this feature brief: "Add a way for grant admins to communicate with individual employees about their ESOP status."

Phase 1 — Divergent (45 minutes): Generate 12 ideas. Literally any idea. In-app chat? Push notifications? Email digest? AI-generated status summaries? Scheduled video call integration? Slack integration? A physical letter template generator? A public announcement board? Write everything. No filtering. No judgment.

Phase 2 — Converge (45 minutes): Now add the real constraints: the team has 1 developer available for 2 weeks, the product is B2B SaaS with enterprise clients who have strict data security requirements, and the PM wants to ship something in 2 weeks. Which 2 of your 12 ideas survive? Why?

Phase 3 — Write the rationale (30 minutes): For the 2 ideas you selected, write the full design rationale formula. Include why you rejected the other 10 ideas explicitly.

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
A PM tells you: "We want to make it easier for HR managers to export ESOP data for their quarterly board presentations."

Generate 8 ideas (divergent — ignore constraints). Then apply these constraints: the engineering team has 5 days, the product already has a basic CSV export, and the users are non-technical HR professionals. Converge to 1 idea. Write the rationale.

**Reflection:**
Were you more comfortable in divergent or convergent mode? Most designers who come from agency backgrounds tend to over-converge (they want to deliver fast). Most designers who come from academic UX backgrounds tend to over-diverge (they want to explore everything). Know which bias you have.

**✅ Right direction:** Treating divergent and convergent as two separate phases that you consciously switch between, never mixing them.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Judging ideas while generating them (killing divergent mode), or generating too many ideas without ever committing to one (avoiding convergent mode). Both are ways of avoiding the actual hard part of design work.

### DAY 12: Wireframing as a Thinking Tool

**The Big Idea:** Wireframes are NOT deliverables. They are thinking on paper. A wireframe that looks like a polished black-and-white version of your final UI is not a wireframe — it is a lie. It takes the same cognitive effort as designing the real thing, which defeats the purpose. True wireframing is low-fidelity because your clarity is still low.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read these:
- "How to Translate Business Requirements" transcript — the wireframing section
- "Stop Making IA Sitemaps" transcript — the story-first approach

The key line from Anudeep: "Low fidelity doesn't mean sketches alone. Low fidelity means you have low clarity about whether it works. A more sorted design, more sorted UI = high clarity." Fidelity is about clarity, not about visual quality.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take the emotion-first ideas you generated on Day 10. Pick 2 of them. For each idea:

Round 1 (15 minutes per idea, rough sketching only — paper or basic Figma with rectangles and text): Sketch 3 completely different structural approaches to that idea. No real components. No styling. Just boxes and labels. The goal is to externalize 3 genuinely different architectural decisions, not 3 slightly different visual treatments.

Round 2 (30 minutes per idea): Choose one approach from Round 1. Add one more layer of clarity — slightly more specific layout, which key information appears where, what the primary action is. Still no real UI.

Round 3 (reflection): Write one paragraph per idea: what did the sketching process reveal that you didn't know before you started? What decision became clear because you put it on paper?

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Take the Zepto daily essentials feature from Anudeep's hot seat review. The architectural problem he identified: when two kits both contain the same item (e.g., both morning kit and evening kit have milk), what happens in the cart?

Sketch 3 different architectural approaches to resolving this conflict:
1. Approach A: Kits are separate cart entities — items in one kit don't merge with items in another
2. Approach B: Kits merge into a single cart — duplicate items are consolidated automatically
3. Approach C: User is prompted when a conflict is detected and asked to choose

For each approach, write one sentence on the tradeoff: what does this approach make easier for the user, and what does it make harder?

**Reflection:**
Did you find yourself wanting to skip to high-fidelity Figma work? That pull is the design equivalent of wanting to start typing code before you know what you are building. The pull is real. The discipline is in resisting it.

**✅ Right direction:** Using the roughest possible representation of an idea at its earliest stage, and only adding fidelity as your clarity increases.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Making "wireframes" that are black-and-white versions of your final UI. This is called cargo-cult wireframing. It looks like process but produces no additional clarity. Interviewers immediately recognize it.

### DAY 13: Information Architecture — Story First, Always

**The Big Idea:** Information architecture cannot be represented in a sitemap. IA is the architecture of experience that emerges naturally when you think story-first. If you can tell the story of the experience, the IA will reveal itself. If you draw the IA first, you will get lost in mathematical combinations of roads and never find the actual experience.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read:
- "Stop Making IA Sitemaps" transcript in full

Notice the teaching technique: Anudeep keeps saying "tell me the story" instead of "show me the architecture." Every time a student tries to describe the structure, he redirects: "Tell it like a movie. One user. One journey. What happens?"

The moment the student tells the story, the architecture starts emerging naturally — because the story forces you to make decisions about what exists, what comes first, what the user discovers when, and what they need to trust the product. A sitemap cannot force you to make those decisions.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take a product idea you want to design: either one from your assignment bank, or a new concept for HR tech. Tell its core story in 500 words.

Rules:
- One user. One specific goal. One journey.
- Write it in first person (as if you are the user)
- Include the moment of discovery, the moment of confusion, the moment of trust, and the moment of completion
- Do NOT write about screens. Write about what the user sees, feels, and decides.

After writing the story, extract the IA decisions it forced you to make. You should find at minimum 5 architectural decisions embedded in the story — decisions about what exists in the product, how it is organized, and what the user discovers when.

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
A PM gives you this brief: "We want to build a feature where managers can track their team's ESOP vesting progress across the year and send updates to team members."

Step 1: Write the full user story (300 words, story format, one manager, one scenario).
Step 2: Extract the 5 IA decisions the story forces you to make.
Step 3: Write which of those decisions is the hardest one (the one with the most trade-offs) and why.
Step 4: Sketch the one screen that is the heart of this feature — the screen where the manager's most important action happens.

**Reflection:**
Where in the story did you find yourself wanting to jump to a technical solution? That jump is the point where your product thinking stops and your execution mode starts. The quality of your designs depends on staying in story mode longer before jumping to execution mode.

**✅ Right direction:** Starting every new product design challenge with a story of one user going through one scenario. Letting the structure emerge from the story.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Starting with a sitemap or navigation structure before you have a story. This produces technically complete but experientially empty products. The architecture is correct, but nobody wants to use it.

**The Big Idea:** Everything from this week — the audit, root cause thinking, emotion-first ideation, divergent/convergent modes, wireframing, story-first IA — all of it leads to one thing: designing the right thing, not just designing the thing right.

**Study Session (2 hours):**

### DAY 15: Lean Research — How to Get Real Insights Without Budget or Time

**The Big Idea:** Most junior designers think research means a 3-month formal study with 50 participants, a dedicated UXR team, and a big report. That is not research. That is academia. Real product company research is lean, fast, and directional. 5 users will reveal 85% of your usability issues. You do not need more.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read:
- research-lean.md reference document in full
- The hot seat reviews where user research is praised — specifically Salony's insight about users going to Instagram for skincare guidance (from the Nykaa project)

Notice what made Salony's insight powerful: she didn't just observe that users were dropping off. She understood WHY — they were leaving because they needed guidance that the app wasn't providing. That insight came from 3 user interviews, not a 200-person study. That is lean research done well.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Today you will do a real 5-second test on your own portfolio or an existing project.

Choose one screen from your ESOP project or any project. Show it to 3 different people for exactly 5 seconds each. After the 5 seconds, ask:
- What is this page about?
- What would you do next on this screen?
- What did you notice first?
- What felt confusing, if anything?

Write every response verbatim. Do not paraphrase. After all 3 tests, synthesize: what did all 3 people agree on? What did they disagree on? What surprised you? What confirmed what you already suspected?

This takes about 45 minutes total. The remaining 2+ hours: write the 5 interview questions you would use if you had 30 minutes with one of your target users. Each question must uncover emotional behavior, not feature preferences. "What do you do when you can't find something in the system?" is a better question than "What features do you want?"

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Design a lean research plan for this scenario: You are joining a new company in your first week. The PM says "we're planning to redesign the employee onboarding flow. Can you do some research before we start?" You have 3 days.

Write:
- Which 2 lean research methods would you use? (Choose from: guerrilla testing, 5-second test, stakeholder interviews, analytics review, session recording review)
- Who would you talk to? (Specific types of people — not "users" but "new employees who joined in the last 3 months")
- What are your 5 interview questions?
- What would tell you your hypothesis is CORRECT?
- What would tell you your hypothesis is WRONG? (This second question is critical — most juniors never ask it)
- What would you present to the PM on Day 3?

**Reflection:**
Have you ever done real research — talking to actual users, watching real people use a product? If not, plan to do it this week on any app. Not for an assignment. Just to build the muscle. Watch one person use Swiggy for 5 minutes without helping them. You will see 3 usability problems in 5 minutes that no heuristic audit would have found.

**✅ Right direction:** Doing imperfect, lean research that gives you directional insights you can act on within a week.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Skipping research because "we don't have time" or doing fake research (showing your design to one colleague and calling it a "usability test"). Both lead to the same outcome: designs that feel right but don't work for real users.

---

### DAY 16: Research Ethics — The Danger of "Users Said So"

**The Big Idea:** Research is to understand root causes, not to justify ideas you already have. The most dangerous phrase in UX design is "users said they want this." It sounds like research. It is actually a way of avoiding responsibility for your design decisions.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read:
- Isha's hot seat review from "What a UX Designer Actually Does" — specifically the moment where Anudeep says "users said so therefore I did it is a big danger zone"
- The research anti-patterns section in research-lean.md
- The section in UX Sensei skill: "Knowing which user pain is real vs. stated"

The key insight: users say "I want more features" but they mean "I feel lost." Users say "I want a search bar" but they mean "I can't find what I need in your current navigation." The words are not the insight. The emotion underneath the words is the insight. That emotion only reveals itself when you listen carefully and ask the right follow-up questions.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take your ESOP project or any HR tech project. Write the story of your research (if you did research). Now apply the "real vs. stated" filter:

For each user insight you used, write:
- What the user literally said
- What you think they actually meant (the emotion underneath)
- How your design addressed the emotion, not just the words
- Whether your design would have been different if you had addressed the stated vs. the real insight

If you didn't do research on that project, write what research you would do now if you could go back, and what questions you would ask differently.

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
You are designing a feature to help ESOP grant admins send reminders to employees who haven't acknowledged their grant documents. You interview 5 users and they all say: "We want an automated email reminder system."

Write:
1. What is the literal stated want?
2. What are 3 possible real needs underneath that want?
3. For each real need, write what the ideal design would be (they may be very different from "automated email reminders")
4. Which of the 3 real needs would you investigate further? How?
5. What 2 follow-up questions would you ask the same users to get to the root?

**Reflection:**
Think about a time when you built something someone asked for and it still didn't solve the problem. That gap between "what they asked for" and "what they needed" is the core challenge of UX design. Write about that gap from your own experience.

**✅ Right direction:** Treating user requests as clues to investigate, not specifications to execute.
**❌ Wrong direction:** The reverse is also wrong: ignoring what users say entirely and relying only on your own instinct. Both extremes fail. The right answer is: listen carefully, find the real need, design for that.

### DAY 17: Business Language Toolkit — How to Talk to PMs and Stakeholders

**The Big Idea:** The reason most designers feel like they are not taken seriously in meetings is not because their designs are bad. It is because they are speaking a different language. Learn to speak business — not by abandoning design thinking, but by translating it.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read:
- stakeholder-communication.md reference (full document, re-read everything)
- "How does a UX Designer work" transcript — the KPI question section
- "Harsh Truth About Business" transcript — the "explaining business value" section

The most important thing to internalize: the moment you ask "what metric is this trying to move?" you change the entire dynamic of the relationship. The PM stops seeing you as someone who makes pretty screens and starts seeing you as someone who helps the business succeed.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Simulate 3 different stakeholder conversations. Write these out as realistic dialogues, not bullet points:

Conversation 1: A PM gives you a vague brief ("make the dashboard better"). Write the full conversation — how you redirect to business metrics, what questions you ask, and what you agree to deliver.

Conversation 2: A PM pushes back on your design recommendation ("we don't have time for all that, just make the button bigger"). Write the full conversation — how you acknowledge their constraint, explain the risk, and propose a middle path.

Conversation 3: You are presenting a completed design and a senior stakeholder says "I don't think users will use this." Write the full conversation — how you respond using research + rationale + metric, not defensiveness.

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Write your personal "business language cheat sheet" — 10 phrases you will use in your first 3 months at a new company. Each phrase should:
- Replace a vague or defensive design-speak phrase
- Use business language clearly
- Not sound robotic or forced

Example: Replace "I think this works better visually" with "This layout reduces the number of steps to the conversion action by 2, which should improve completion rate."

Write 10 of these translations for your specific context (HR tech or product company).

**Reflection:**
Think about a moment in your previous job where you felt your design was rejected or dismissed. Rewrite how that conversation could have gone if you had framed your recommendation in business language. Be specific. This is not just an exercise in hindsight — it is building the muscle for future situations.

**✅ Right direction:** Entering every stakeholder conversation with the metric in mind. "This design should move [metric] by [mechanism]."
**❌ Wrong direction:** Defending your design by saying "it's a better user experience." That sentence has no weight in a business meeting because it cannot be measured or disputed. It sounds like opinion, not evidence.

### DAY 18: The Design Rationale Formula — Deep Practice

**The Big Idea:** The design rationale formula is not a writing exercise. It is a way of thinking. If you cannot complete the formula for a decision, it means you made the decision without fully understanding it. The formula forces clarity.

Formula: "I chose [X] because [user need], supported by [evidence/principle]. The alternative was [Y] but it has [trade-off]. The expected outcome is [Z metric impact]."

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Re-read stakeholder-communication.md — specifically the design rationale section and the presentation section. Pay attention to the examples. Notice how specific they are. Not "better user experience" — specific metric, specific mechanism, specific trade-off considered.

Also re-read "Screens Aren't Enough in a UX Portfolio" transcript — specifically the section about how clarity from 20 iterations is more valuable than the final design itself. What stakeholders are buying is confidence in your thinking, not certainty in the outcome.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Go back to your 5 design decisions from Day 5. Write each one using the formula again — but this time, add two more elements:
- One thing that would have changed your decision (what evidence would have made you choose the alternative?)
- One question you would ask before the design ships (what do you still not know?)

This is senior-level thinking. Junior designers write rationale for decisions they've already made. Senior designers write rationale that acknowledges the limits of their own knowledge.

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
Take one design decision from your current ESOP case study. Write:
1. The rationale using the full formula (3-4 sentences)
2. The alternative you considered and why you rejected it
3. What you were uncertain about and how you handled the uncertainty
4. What you would do differently if you were starting over
5. How you would present this decision in a 60-second verbal explanation

Practice reading the 60-second verbal explanation out loud 3 times. Record yourself on the third time. Listen back. Notice: do you sound confident or uncertain? Do you start with the user or with the screen?

**Reflection:**
The decisions that are hardest to write rationale for are the ones you made on feel rather than evidence. These are not necessarily bad decisions — experienced designers make great intuition-based decisions. But you need to be able to reconstruct the reasoning, because in interviews and in design reviews, "it felt right" is not a defensible answer.

**✅ Right direction:** Treating every design decision as a hypothesis that you can explain and potentially be wrong about.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Writing rationale that sounds confident but is actually circular. "I chose this layout because it works better for users" is not a rationale. It is a restatement of the goal, not an explanation of the decision.

### DAY 19: Scale Planning Deep Dive — Your Complete Framework

**The Big Idea:** This is the day you build your personal scale-based process framework — the one you will pull out every time a PM gives you a new task, so you can immediately know how to approach it.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Re-read the Project Scale Framework from Day 6. Then read the "How to understand UX Process" transcript again — specifically the part about building your own practice, not following a standard process.

Add to what you learned on Day 6: the key difference between scales is not just time. It is also about how much you can afford to be wrong. In a 1-day sprint, being wrong costs you 1 day. In a 2.5-month project, being wrong at Month 1 costs you 10 weeks of work. That asymmetry of risk is why you need more research and validation at larger scales.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Build your personal Scale-Based Process Card. This is a document you will actually use. Create 4 cards — one for each scale.

For each scale, define:
- When to use this scale (what signals tell you this is the right mode?)
- What you must always do, regardless of constraints
- What you should do if time allows
- What you can skip at this scale
- What to deliver at the end
- How to present the output
- What to say to a PM who pushes back on the process

Write this for your own context — B2B SaaS, HR tech, product-led growth companies. Make it specific enough that you could hand it to a version of yourself who is starting their first day at a new job.

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
Your new PM at a 200-person Series C startup approaches you on your second week. She says:

"We have three things on the roadmap right now. First, there's a quick fix — the file upload on the expense claim form is broken on mobile. Second, we're redesigning the time-off request flow, which should take about 2 weeks. Third, we're planning a major revamp of the entire manager dashboard — that's a 3-month project."

Write your approach to each of the three:
- The file upload fix: what do you do and what do you NOT do?
- The time-off redesign: what does your 2-week plan look like, day by day?
- The manager dashboard: what does your 3-month plan look like, month by month?
- For each, what do you communicate to the PM about what you need from them?

**Reflection:**
Where in this exercise did you feel most at ease and where did you feel uncertain? That map tells you where your instincts are strong and where you need more practice.

**✅ Right direction:** Treating every new task as a scoping question first. "What scale is this? What process does that scale require?"
**❌ Wrong direction:** Applying the same process to every task regardless of scale. This is the equivalent of using a sledgehammer to hang a picture frame. It wastes time, overwhelms the team, and signals that you don't understand how product teams actually work.

### DAY 20: Scale Planning Applied — Live Practice

**The Big Idea:** Framework without practice is just theory. Today you use the framework on real scenarios, as fast as possible.

**Study Session (1 hour):**

**Practice Session (4 hours):**

1. "Add a CSV export to the ESOP grants table." PM wants it done this week.
2. "Our manager dashboard has very low engagement — barely 20% of managers open it more than once a week." You have 3 weeks.
3. "We want to launch a completely new feature: an AI-assisted benchmarking tool that suggests salary ranges." You have 3 months.
4. "The mobile app has a crash on the login screen. 3 complaints today." PM is panicking. You have the afternoon.
5. "We want to improve the overall employee experience on the platform. Run a discovery sprint." No timeline given.
6. "I saw that Rippling has this cool visualization of org charts. Can we add something like that?" PM is excited. No timeline given.

- Propose a 2-day "scoping sprint" with a specific output
- Set expectations for what happens after the scoping sprint

**Reflection:**
The scenario that felt most stressful — note it. Stress in a planning exercise usually points to where your instincts are least developed. Give that scenario extra attention this week.

**✅ Right direction:** Responding to ambiguous briefs by scoping them collaboratively, not by either accepting all ambiguity or refusing to start without perfect clarity.
**❌ Wrong direction:** Saying "yes" to every brief as stated. Every "yes" without scoping is a promise you might not be able to keep. Every "I need more clarity" without an alternative is a blocker. The right answer is always: "Here's what I understood, here's what I need from you, here's what I'll deliver by when."

### DAY 22: The 5 Case Study Formats — Understanding the System

**The Big Idea:** Most designers have one case study format (the long Notion document). Companies evaluate you across 5 different formats at 5 different stages of the hiring process. If you are missing any of them, you are losing candidates before they even see your best work.

**Study Session (2 hours):**

The 5 formats:
1. Long-form document case study — for the scrutiny phase (deep reads, portfolio rounds)
2. Presentation deck case study — for live portfolio presentations
3. Video case study — for passive LinkedIn discovery (inbound leads)
4. Micro case studies — for weekly LinkedIn activity (sustained visibility)
5. Verbal case study — for every stage of the interview (this is the actual hiring decision)

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Take your ESOP project. Map out what you currently have vs. what you are missing:
- Long-form: Do you have it? Is it written with decision stories or with templates?
- Presentation deck: Do you have a 10-slide version ready?

- Micro case studies: Have you ever posted a single decision story from this project?
- Verbal: Can you explain this project in 1 minute, 5 minutes, and 20 minutes?

For the formats you are missing, write a 3-sentence plan for how you will build them this week.

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Write one micro case study from your ESOP project. Max 150 words. Must include:
- The specific decision (not the whole project)
- What you chose NOT to do and why (this is what makes it interesting)

Post it or prepare it for posting. Write the hook line — the first sentence that makes someone stop scrolling.

**Reflection:**
The verbal case study is the one most people are most afraid of. The fear comes from the feeling that you might "get it wrong." You cannot get it wrong — because the verbal case study is a story about your thinking, and no one else was in your head. The only way to fail is to not be prepared. Preparation is the antidote.

### DAY 23: Decision Stories — The Core of Every Good Case Study

**The Big Idea:** The thing recruiters are looking for in your case study is this: given a situation, can this person make a good decision? Not a perfect decision — a well-reasoned one. Decision stories are the format that proves this.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Re-read the hot seat reviews — specifically:
- Anudeep's feedback on IRCTC (Anuraj) about showcasing the AI decision story
- His feedback on Pratika's Ola project about showcasing the "repeat use case as the happy path" decision
- His feedback on Salony's project about the discoverability decision (why she placed the quiz in search, not homepage)

In every review, the moment the student explains a design decision with a clear rationale — the situation, the choice made, the reasoning — Anudeep's tone shifts immediately. He says "very impressed" or "now we're talking." Decision stories are the difference between a junior-looking portfolio and a senior-looking one.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Write 3 decision stories from your work. Each one follows this structure:
- Situation: What was the context? What problem were you trying to solve?
- Options: What were the 2-3 design options you considered?
- Decision: What did you choose?
- Reasoning: Why this option and not the others? (Use evidence, principle, or specific user insight)
- Outcome: What happened? What did you learn?

Each story should take 200-300 words. They should read like a story — with tension (the problem), a choice (the decision), and a resolution (the outcome).

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
Rewrite the opening section of your ESOP case study entirely in decision story format. Remove all process language ("then I did research, then I wireframed"). Replace it with 3 decision stories that carry the reader from the problem to the solution through a series of choices you made.

The test: can someone read this opening without any prior context and understand what the project was about, what the hardest parts were, and how you thought through them?

**Reflection:**
Which decision story was hardest to write? Usually it is the one where you made a good decision by instinct but can't fully explain why. This is normal. The skill is learning to reconstruct the reasoning after the fact, which helps you make better conscious decisions next time.

### DAY 24: Scan-Worthy Portfolio Design

**The Big Idea:** Your portfolio is being evaluated in two phases: a 5-second scan and a 30-minute deep read. Most portfolios are built entirely for the deep read and fail at the scan. If you fail the scan, the deep read never happens.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read:
- "5 Case Study Formats" transcript — the scan vs. scrutiny section
- "How to Attract UIUX Recruiters Without Applying" transcript
- The IRCTC, Pratika, Salony, Shanta, and Isha portfolio scan feedbacks from the hot seat reviews

Notice the pattern in every scan feedback Anudeep gives: he always asks "what does this project tell me in the first 5 seconds?" If the answer is "not much," the project is not scan-worthy, regardless of how much work went into it.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Apply the scan test to your own portfolio. Ask someone who has not seen your portfolio before to look at it for 30 seconds and then tell you:
- What do you do?
- What was the most interesting project?
- What company or type of company do you think would hire this person?

If they can answer all 3 clearly — your scan layer is working. If they hesitate or give a generic answer — you have a scan problem.

Then: rewrite your portfolio headline and the case study header for your ESOP project. The header must communicate: what was the product, what was the challenge, and what was your specific contribution — in under 20 words.

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Write the "scan layer" for your ESOP project: everything a recruiter should understand in the first 10 seconds of looking at your case study. This includes:
- Project title (specific and outcome-focused, not generic)
- A 2-sentence summary (the challenge + what you did, no fluff)
- One visual that immediately shows the most compelling part of your solution
- One data point or outcome that makes the work feel real

**Reflection:**
The hardest part of scan layer design is restraint. You want to show everything. The scan layer requires you to choose the single most compelling thing and lead with that. What is the single most impressive thing about your ESOP project? That is what should appear first.

### DAY 25: Stakeholder Pushback — How to Hold Your Ground Without Burning Bridges

**The Big Idea:** Pushback is not confrontation. It is steering toward the right outcome for both the user and the business. The skill is knowing how to disagree productively — not defensively, not submissively.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read:
- stakeholder-communication.md — the pushback section specifically
- The section on "Handling 'Just Make It Pop'"
- "Harsh Truth About Business" — the section on constraints vs. ideology

The magic formula for any pushback: Respect the idea + Explain the risk + Propose a test.

"That's worth exploring — the intention makes sense. The risk with [X] is [Y] based on [evidence or principle]. I'd suggest we try [Z] and measure [metric] to see which direction works better."

This formula does three things: it shows you respect the PM's judgment, it shows you understand the business risk, and it proposes a forward path that doesn't require anyone to be wrong.

**Practice Session (3 hours):**
Write 8 common pushback scenarios you might face in your first 6 months at a new company. For each:
- Write the PM's statement exactly as it might come
- Write the wrong response (agreeing immediately or pushing back defensively)
- Write the right response using the formula

Scenarios to include:
1. "Can you make the CTA more prominent? Just make it brighter."
2. "Marketing wants to add 3 more banners to the dashboard."
3. "The CEO saw this feature on a competitor's product and wants it by next week."
4. "We don't have time for user testing. Just ship it."
5. "The design looks too minimal — users won't understand it."
6. "Can we add more onboarding tooltips? Users are complaining they don't know what to do."
7. "This redesign is going to take too long. Can you just do a quick fix?"
8. "I showed the designs to my wife and she didn't understand them."

**Daily Assignment — SHORT:**
Your PM says: "I showed your proposed ESOP dashboard redesign to the CTO and he said it looks too different from what we have. He doesn't want to alienate existing users. Can we keep it closer to the original?"

Write your full verbal response. It must:
- Acknowledge the concern (not dismiss it)
- Explain the user research or principle that drove your design direction
- Propose a path that respects both the business concern and the design intent
- End with a specific next step, not an open question

**Reflection:**
The fear of pushback is usually not about being wrong — it is about conflict. Most people avoid conflict even when they know their position is right. Building the courage to advocate for your design (with evidence, not emotion) is one of the most career-defining skills you can develop in your mid-career years.

### DAY 26: The Verbal Case Study — Your Real Interview Weapon

**The Big Idea:** The verbal case study is the most important format and the most neglected one. In every stage of the hiring process — phone screen, portfolio round, culture fit round — someone will ask you to "tell me about your work." Your answer in that moment is the verbal case study. Practice is the only thing that makes it good.

**Study Session (2 hours):**
Read:
- "5 Case Study Formats" transcript — verbal case study section
- "What To Say In UX Job Interview Without Real Work Experience" transcript
- The Anudeep story about how he got hired at his first startup (the selling yourself section)

The key insight: a verbal case study is a performance with a structure, not a summary of your written case study. It has a hook (something that grabs attention immediately), a conflict (the hardest design problem you faced), a decision (how you resolved it), and an impact (what changed). These four elements in 60 seconds or in 30 minutes.

**Practice Session (4 hours):**
Build and rehearse 3 versions of your ESOP verbal case study:

Version 1 — 60 seconds: Hook + one decision + one outcome. Practice this until it is automatic. This is your phone screen answer.

Version 2 — 5 minutes: Hook + user context + 2 key decisions with rationale + outcome + what you'd improve. This is your "tell me about a project" answer in a first round.

Version 3 — 15 minutes: Full story — business context, user journey, 4 key design decisions with trade-offs, research insights, what you tried and abandoned, what shipped, how you'd measure it, what the next version looks like. This is your portfolio round presentation.

For each version: write the full script, then practice reading it aloud, then practice without notes. Record the 5-minute version. Listen back. Note where you lose energy or confidence — those are the moments you need to rebuild with better storytelling.

**Daily Assignment — MEDIUM:**
Write your complete 60-second verbal case study for your ESOP project. Post it here as text. Then write what you would ADD if given 5 minutes (not repeat — add). Then write what you would add if given 15 minutes.

**Reflection:**
Most people speak about their work in the wrong order. They start with "so we had this product" and describe the context for 3 minutes before anything interesting happens. The hook needs to come first. "I designed a grant management system that reduced processing time from 23 days to 10 days at enterprise scale — here's how I got there." That one sentence is more powerful than 2 minutes of context-setting.

# WHAT AI HELPS WITH (AND WHAT IT DOESN'T)

Use AI to accelerate these tasks — but never to replace the human thinking behind them:

**Use AI for:**
- Generating first-draft design rationale when you know the decision but are struggling to articulate it
- Stress-testing your design logic ("here is my reasoning, what are the weakest points?")
- Generating edge cases you might have missed
- Writing the first draft of any stakeholder communication document
- Comparing two design approaches against UX principles
- Writing UX copy for screens quickly
- Researching competitor patterns and industry benchmarks

**Do NOT use AI for:**
- Generating user personas and treating them as research (they are not — they are assumptions dressed up as data)
- Deciding which design direction to go (that judgment is yours)
- Replacing actual conversations with real users
- Writing decision stories for decisions you haven't actually made
- Generating "insights" you haven't earned through observation

The rule is simple: AI is a thinking partner, not a thinking replacement. Every time you use AI to help with a deliverable, you should be able to explain the output in your own words without looking at it. If you can't — the output isn't yours yet.
