Quick Reference

The most important principles, formulas, and frameworks — synthesized from all 30 days

The 15 Most Important Principles

Memorize and apply these daily. From the transcripts — the principles Anudeep returns to most often.

01

Experience design is about emotions in a journey, not elements on a screen.

The Manu framework: UX is the holistic experience of a human being across time. Every screen decision should be traced back to an emotional moment in the journey. If you cannot name the emotion you are designing for, you are designing blind.

→ Day 1
02

Your job is to turn ambiguity into clarity. Any input is valid. Every output should have more clarity than the input.

The UX designer's core value proposition is not visual design or wireframing — it is the ability to take a vague problem and produce a clear, actionable direction. A PM who says "improve the dashboard" should leave a meeting with you holding a specific, scoped, metric-connected brief.

→ Day 2
03

Every design decision should be answerable with the rationale formula: I chose X because [user need], supported by [evidence]. The alternative was Y, but [trade-off]. The outcome should be [metric impact].

The design rationale formula is not just for documentation — it is the internal check you run before committing to any design direction. If you cannot complete the formula, you do not understand your own decision well enough to defend it.

→ Day 18
04

The best UX ideas come from precisely mapping the user's emotional state at a specific moment, not from generic brainstorming.

Emotion-first ideation: instead of asking "how can we improve this screen?", ask "what emotion is the user feeling at this exact moment, and what would make that emotion better?" The emotion is the brief. The screen is the response.

→ Day 4
05

Root causes are almost never the same as surface symptoms. Ask "why" at least 3 times before designing.

The 3-why technique: when you encounter a problem, ask why it exists. When you get an answer, ask why again. Three iterations typically surface the actual root cause — which is almost always a structural, emotional, or business problem rather than a visual or interaction problem.

→ Day 9
06

Wireframes are low-fidelity because clarity is low — not because they need to look rough. Fidelity = clarity.

The appropriate fidelity level for any design deliverable is determined by how clearly you understand the problem and solution — not by convention or workflow tradition. If you are certain about the structure and uncertain only about visual details, go straight to high-fidelity. If you are uncertain about structure, stay low-fidelity until clarity is achieved.

→ Day 12
07

Information architecture emerges from stories, not from sitemaps. Tell the story first.

The story-first IA method: write the complete narrative of one user accomplishing their primary goal. Every noun in the story is a potential content type. Every verb is a potential action or navigation element. The structure of the story reveals the structure of the product — far more accurately than any top-down sitemap exercise.

→ Day 13
08

Five users reveal 85% of usability problems. Research does not require scale — it requires specificity.

The lean research principle: you do not need a research budget, a lab, or 50 participants to get actionable insight. Three to five specific, well-recruited users in a focused session will surface the vast majority of usability problems. Research quality comes from asking the right questions of the right people — not from sample size alone.

→ Day 15
09

"Users said they want X" is not a design rationale. "Users feel [specific emotion] at [specific moment], which makes them seek [stated desire] as a way to resolve it" is a design rationale.

Research ethics: users report what they can articulate, not what drives their behavior. The stated desire ("I want a search bar") is often a surface expression of a deeper need ("I feel lost and can't find what I'm looking for"). Your job is to design for the underlying need, not the stated solution.

→ Day 16
10

Scale your process to the stakes. A 1-day sprint needs heuristics and empathy. A 3-month project needs research, iteration, and validation.

The scale planning framework: the most common design process mistake is applying a fixed process to every problem regardless of timeline or stakes. A 4-hour emergency fix and a 3-month platform redesign require completely different levels of research depth, iteration cycles, and documentation. Match your process to the stakes.

→ Day 19
11

The hiring decision is made in conversation, not in reading. Build the verbal case study first.

The verbal case study is the most practiced and least prepared format. Every other portfolio asset builds toward it — but most designers only practice the written version. The moment "tell me about your work" is asked in an interview, the 30-second verbal hook determines whether the conversation continues with interest or polite obligation.

→ Day 26
12

Inbound leads have a dramatically higher conversion rate than outbound applications. Build visibility, not just applications.

When a recruiter finds you through LinkedIn, a referral, or your content — they approach with a pre-existing positive impression. The conversion rate from inbound interest to offer is significantly higher than from cold application. Build the inbound engine through specific, consistent, valuable content — even while actively applying.

→ Day 27
13

Every negative emotion in a user journey is a gap to address. Every positive emotion is an opportunity to amplify. Design for both.

Most UX work focuses on fixing pain — removing friction, reducing errors, simplifying flows. The full opportunity space includes amplifying delight — the moments where users feel confident, capable, or excited. The best products do both. Map the full emotional arc, not just the pain points.

→ Day 4
14

The question that repositions you in any PM meeting: "What business metric is this trying to move?"

When a PM presents a vague brief or a feature request without a metric, asking this question immediately reframes the conversation from "what should we build" to "what are we trying to achieve." It positions you as a strategic partner rather than an execution resource — and it prevents you from designing the right solution to the wrong problem.

→ Day 3
15

Designing for the right problem is worth more than designing the right solution to the wrong problem.

Problem definition is 80% of the design work. The most common failure mode for mid-level designers is producing excellent design solutions for problems that were never properly diagnosed. Root cause thinking, business metric framing, and user journey mapping all exist to ensure you are solving the right problem before committing design resources to a solution.

→ Day 9

Key Formulas

These structures recur throughout the 30 days. Internalize them until they become automatic.

Design Rationale Formula
"I chose [X] because [user need], supported by [evidence]. The alternative was [Y], but [trade-off]. The expected outcome is [metric impact]."
Use whenever: defending a design decision in review, writing case study content, answering "why did you do this?" in an interview.
Pushback Formula
"That's worth exploring — the intention makes sense. The risk with [X] is [Y] based on [evidence or principle]. I'd suggest we try [Z] and measure [metric] to see which direction works better."
Structure: Respect the idea → Explain the specific risk → Propose a testable forward path.
Stakeholder Presentation Structure
30s — Business problem (in metric language) · 60s — User journey + 2 key emotional moments · 90s — 3 key design decisions with rationale · 60s — How you'd measure success · 30s — What you'd improve next
Total: ~4.5 minutes. Scales up by adding more decision depth. Always opens with the business problem, never the screen.
Verbal Case Study Structure
Hook — the most compelling thing in one sentence · Business context — who is the user, what was at stake · Key decision — the hardest design choice · Reasoning — why this and not the alternatives · Outcome — what changed
Works in 60 seconds. At 5 minutes, add 2–3 decisions. At 20 minutes, walk the full arc with research, iterations, and retrospective.
Decision Story Structure
Situation — the specific moment where a decision was needed · Options — the real alternatives you considered · Decision — what you chose · Reasoning — why this and not the others · Outcome — what changed and what you learned
Use in: case study writing, verbal presentations, LinkedIn micro posts. 200–300 words per story. Non-linear order — start with the most interesting moment.
3-Layer UX Audit
Layer 1 — Usability (5-second test, navigation clarity, error prevention) · Layer 2 — Heuristics (Nielsen's 10 principles, consistency, visibility of status) · Layer 3 — Business Alignment (does each screen move the metric it should move?)
Always audit in this order — Layer 1 problems invalidate Layer 2 and 3 analysis. Connect every finding to a specific business metric.

What AI Helps With (And What It Doesn't)

Use AI to accelerate — never to replace the human thinking behind the output.

✅ Use AI for
  • Generating first-draft design rationale when you know the decision but are struggling to articulate it
  • Stress-testing your design logic ("here is my reasoning, what are the weakest points?")
  • Generating edge cases you might have missed
  • Writing the first draft of any stakeholder communication document
  • Comparing two design approaches against UX principles
  • Writing UX copy for screens quickly
  • Researching competitor patterns and industry benchmarks
❌ Don't use AI for
  • Generating user personas and treating them as research (they are assumptions dressed up as data)
  • Deciding which design direction to go (that judgment is yours)
  • Replacing actual conversations with real users
  • Writing decision stories for decisions you haven't actually made
  • Generating "insights" you haven't earned through observation
💡

"AI is a thinking partner, not a thinking replacement. Every time you use AI to help with a deliverable, you should be able to explain the output in your own words without looking at it. If you can't — the output isn't yours yet."
