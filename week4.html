The 5 Case Study Formats — The System

Why One Format Is Not Enough

A portfolio case study is not a single asset. It is a system of 5 different assets that serve 5 different moments in the hiring process. Most designers build one (the long Notion document) and wonder why they are not getting interviews despite excellent work.

The problem: different stages of the hiring process require different types of engagement. A recruiter doing initial screening does not have 30 minutes to read a case study — they have 30 seconds to decide whether to move forward. A senior design manager in a portfolio round has the time and the motivation to read every word you have written. A LinkedIn-browsing recruiter who has not posted a job yet is in a completely passive state — they need something that stops their scroll without them knowing why.

Each of these scenarios requires a different format. Build all 5.

The Scan Phase vs. The Scrutiny Phase

Before diving into the 5 formats, understand the two-phase model of how hiring decisions are made.

The scan phase: recruiters are looking at your portfolio quickly, deciding whether you are worth a deeper look. In this phase, they are not reading your case study — they are skimming, scanning, and pattern-matching. They are asking: "Does this person seem credible? Do their projects seem relevant? Is there anything here that makes me want to read more?" The entire scan phase takes less than 60 seconds.

If you fail the scan phase, the scrutiny phase never happens. Your deep, beautifully written case study never gets read.

The scrutiny phase: you have passed the scan. Now the hiring manager is going deep. They are reading everything. They are looking for every reason NOT to hire you as much as they are looking for reasons to hire you. They will catch every undefended decision, every template-copied structure, every claim that does not hold up under a question.

Most designers write their case studies entirely for the scrutiny phase. They write detailed, thorough documents that are impenetrable in the scan. Build both layers.

The 5 Formats — What Each Is and When It Works

Format 1: The Long-Form Document Case Study
What: a comprehensive written account of the project on Notion, Medium, or a personal website. 1,500–3,000 words. Includes business context, user research insights, design decisions with rationale, iterations, outcomes.
When it works: scrutiny phase. Portfolio rounds. When a hiring manager has specifically allocated 30 minutes to understand your work.
Critical tip: do NOT follow a template. The moment your case study reads like "Problem → Research → Wireframes → Final Design," a senior reader immediately recognizes the formula and discounts your original thinking. Write it as a series of design decision stories, not a process recap. The reader wants to know how you think, not how a template works.

Format 2: The Presentation Deck Case Study
What: a 10–15 slide deck version of the same project. Highlights and images. Built for live verbal presentation.
When it works: portfolio presentation rounds where you walk the interviewer through your work. The deck supports your verbal explanation — you speak more than it shows.
Critical tip: when presenting, give the interviewer your prototype link before you begin. Ask them to use the product on their phone for 2 minutes before you start talking. They will experience the product as users before they hear your explanation as a designer. This transforms the conversation from "here is what I built" to "here is why I made the choices that produced what you just experienced."

Format 3: The Video Case Study
What: a 60–90 second video where you hold your phone in one hand and walk through the prototype while explaining what is happening and why.
When it works: LinkedIn and Instagram discovery. Passive inbound. Recruiters who are browsing, not actively searching.
Critical tip: do NOT record a screen recording with a voiceover. That feels like a product demo. Instead: hold the phone visibly in your hand, tap through the prototype, and talk to the camera as if explaining to a friend. Human, conversational, specific. This video stops scroll because it looks like someone passionate about real work, not a polished product demo.

Format 4: Micro Case Studies
What: a single design decision from your project, written as a 150–250 word LinkedIn post with a hook line and a question at the end.
When it works: sustained LinkedIn presence. Weekly touchpoints that keep you visible without requiring you to produce full case studies constantly.
Why it works: a sea of applicants is posting full case studies and hoping someone reads 2,000 words. You are posting one interesting decision in 200 words. "When I was designing the grant management table, I had two options for the confirmation flow: a modal or a separate page. Here's why I chose the modal and what I would change about that decision now..." That is a post a senior designer stops to read.

Format 5: The Verbal Case Study
What: a completely rehearsed, fluent verbal explanation of your project that you can deliver in 60 seconds, 5 minutes, or 20 minutes, with zero visual support.
When it works: every single stage of the hiring process. Phone screen. Casual networking. First interview. Portfolio round. Final round. The verbal case study is the actual hiring decision — because "hiring is about the conversation," not the document.
Why it is the most missed format: people practice their written case study. They do not practice speaking about their work until the words come out naturally, confidently, and without notes.
The structure: Hook → Business context → Key decision → Reasoning → Outcome. This works in 60 seconds. At 5 minutes, you add 2–3 decisions. At 20 minutes, you walk the full story.

The verbal case study is the one most people are most afraid of. The fear comes from the feeling that you might "get it wrong." Decision Stories — Writing What Recruiters Actually Want to Read

What Recruiters Are Looking for When They Read Your Case Study

When a senior designer or design manager reads a portfolio case study, they are not evaluating whether you followed the right process. They are asking one question: "Given a situation, can this person make a good design decision?"

They are not hiring you for the project you did. They are hiring you for the projects you will do in their company. The evidence they need is not proof of your past output — it is proof of your present thinking.

Decision stories are the format that provides this proof. They show: you encountered a real design situation, you considered multiple options, you chose one, and you can explain why. This demonstrates active, considered judgment rather than passive execution.

The Decision Story Structure

Every decision story has five elements. They do not need to be labeled. They should flow naturally in your writing.

Situation: what was the context that created the design challenge? Not a summary of the whole project — the specific moment where a decision was needed. "When we were designing the grant acceptance flow, we discovered that 23% of employees were abandoning the process at the document upload step. This was our highest-priority problem because it represented a breakdown in the core compliance workflow."

Options: what were the real alternatives you considered? This is critical. Recruiters know that any decision worth making had alternatives. If you do not mention alternatives, it looks like you executed a single idea without exploring. "We had three options: simplify the upload to accept only scanned PDFs, replace the physical document requirement with an e-signature service, or add more guidance content to the upload step."

Decision: what did you choose? Be specific and direct. "We chose to implement e-signature through a third-party service, which eliminated the upload step entirely."

Reasoning: why this decision and not the others? This is where you demonstrate your thinking quality. "The guidance content approach (option 3) was a surface fix — it would explain a step that should not exist rather than removing the underlying friction. The PDF simplification (option 1) improved the upload mechanics but still required physical document creation, which meant the abandonment would likely persist. E-signature removed the root cause: the requirement for a physical document."

Outcome: what changed? This does not need to be a dramatic success story. An honest "we expected X, we saw Y, and we learned Z" is more credible than a perfect success narrative. "Implementation took 2 sprint cycles. Grant acceptance completion rate improved from 74% to 91%. We also saw an unexpected benefit: the time between grant offer and acceptance fell from an average of 12 days to 3 days, which reduced the workload on the HR team significantly."

The Storytelling Approach — Non-Linear is Better

Most case studies are written chronologically: "First I did this, then I did that, then I discovered this, then I made this change." This mirrors the actual project timeline. It does not mirror how the reader experiences your work.

Consider non-linear storytelling. Start with the most interesting moment — the problem that was hardest to solve, the discovery that changed your direction, the design decision that had the most uncertainty. Hook the reader first. Then build the context. Then show the resolution.

This is how good writing works. A thriller does not open with the detective going to law school. It opens with the crime scene. The backstory comes as you need it.

Applied to your ESOP case study: instead of "The project started when our company needed to redesign the grant management flow due to scaling requirements," consider: "When a grant admin tries to issue 500 grants at once in our old system, the process takes 23 days. The employees on the other end of those grants — people waiting to see ownership that is rightfully theirs — are waiting those 23 days too. This is the problem I spent 4 months solving." Now the reader is in. Context, stakes, human consequence. That is a hook.

🛠️ Practice Session 3 hours

Write 3 decision stories from your work. Each one follows this structure:

  • Situation: What was the context? What problem were you trying to solve?
  • Options: What were the 2–3 design options you considered?
  • Decision: What did you choose?
  • Reasoning: Why this option and not the others? (Use evidence, principle, or specific user insight)
  • Outcome: What happened? What did you learn?

Each story should take 200–300 words. They should read like a story — with tension (the problem), a choice (the decision), and a resolution (the outcome).

Which decision story was hardest to write? Usually it is the one where you made a good decision by instinct but can't fully explain why. This is normal. The skill is learning to reconstruct the reasoning after the fact, which helps you make better conscious decisions next time.

✅ Right Direction ▼

Writing case study content as a series of decision stories — naming the alternatives you considered and explaining why you chose one over the others. This demonstrates active judgment, not passive execution.

❌ Wrong Direction ▼

Writing process recaps: "First I researched, then I wireframed, then I iterated." This tells the reader what you did — not what you thought. Decision stories are the difference between a junior-looking portfolio and a senior-looking one.

Scan-Worthy Portfolio Design

The 10-Second Test

Your portfolio has approximately 10 seconds to answer three questions in the mind of a scanning recruiter:

  1. What does this person do? (Role and expertise)
  2. Is their work relevant to what I need? (Industry, product type, complexity)
  3. Is there something here worth spending more time on? (A project, a headline, a visual that creates curiosity)

If your portfolio fails to answer any of these in 10 seconds, the recruiter moves on. Not because your work is bad — because your portfolio is designed for someone who is already interested, not someone who is deciding whether to become interested.

The Most Common Scan Failures

Generic headline: "I design user-centered experiences that balance business goals with user needs." This is true of every UX designer on the planet. It communicates nothing specific about you. Replace it with something that says what you have actually worked on and what you are specifically good at.

Concept projects leading over real work: when a recruiter sees your portfolio and cannot tell what is real work vs. what you designed in your bedroom, they assume the worst. Always lead with your real company work, regardless of whether the company name is recognizable.

Project titles that describe the project type instead of the outcome: "ESOP Grant Management Redesign" → what this means to a recruiter: "they redesigned something." Better: "Reducing grant processing from 23 days to 10 days — an ESOP platform redesign at scale." The second version communicates the stakes, the outcome, and the complexity.

Heavy case studies that start with process: "I began by conducting a thorough research phase including stakeholder interviews, user interviews, competitive analysis, and..." The recruiter has left. Start with the problem, not the process.

No visual hook: your case study needs at least one image in the scan view that makes a recruiter think "that looks interesting." Not polished for polish's sake — interesting because it communicates something unexpected, specific, or impressive about the solution.

The Inception Portfolio Problem

A real-world feedback observation from multiple portfolio reviews: many designers link to a portfolio within a portfolio, or embed Figma files as their "portfolio link." The recruiter clicks a link, lands in a design tool, and has to navigate to see the work. This is friction. This is loss. Put the work where people land without any navigation required.

Another common mistake: listing your portfolio on one platform, then using a link to a second platform as your actual portfolio. Every additional click is a 20–30% drop in the audience that sees your work.

Make the path from "recruiter sees your LinkedIn" to "recruiter sees your best project" as short as possible. Ideally: one click.

🛠️ Practice Session 3 hours

Apply the scan test to your own portfolio. Ask someone who has not seen your portfolio before to look at it for 30 seconds and then tell you:

  • What do you do?
  • What was the most interesting project?
  • What company or type of company do you think would hire this person?

If they can answer all 3 clearly — your scan layer is working. If they hesitate or give a generic answer — you have a scan problem.

Then: rewrite your portfolio headline and the case study header for your ESOP project. The header must communicate: what was the product, what was the challenge, and what was your specific contribution — in under 20 words.

The hardest part of scan layer design is restraint. You want to show everything. The scan layer requires you to choose the single most compelling thing and lead with that. What is the single most impressive thing about your ESOP project? That is what should appear first.

✅ Right Direction ▼

Designing your portfolio for the scan phase first — leading with a specific outcome-focused headline, a compelling visual, and a 2-sentence summary that communicates stakes. Then building the deep read underneath.

❌ Wrong Direction ▼

Building a portfolio that opens with your process, your methodology, or a generic designer headline. Starting with "I believe in user-centered design" communicates nothing differentiating. Start with what you specifically did and what changed because of it.

Day 25 Stakeholder Pushback — How to Hold Your Ground Without Burning Bridges
SHORT ▼
💡 The Big Idea

Pushback is not confrontation. It is steering toward the right outcome for both the user and the business. The skill is knowing how to disagree productively — not defensively, not submissively.

📖 Study Session 2 hours
📚 Study References

The magic formula for any pushback: Respect the idea + Explain the risk + Propose a test.

Stakeholder Pushback — Holding Your Ground

The Pushback Formula

The most effective response to any stakeholder challenge to your design follows a three-part structure. Internalize this structure until it becomes your automatic response:

Part 1 — Respect the idea, not dismiss it. The stakeholder who challenges your design is not your enemy. They have a perspective that is worth understanding, even if you ultimately disagree. "That's an interesting direction, and I can see the logic in it" is not being dishonest — it is creating the conditions for a productive conversation.

Part 2 — Explain the specific risk, grounded in evidence. Not "I don't think that's a good idea" but "The risk with that approach is [specific user behavior or business consequence]. We know from [research/principle/data] that [specific thing happens in this scenario]." Evidence depersonalizes the disagreement. You are not saying the PM is wrong. You are saying "here is what the data or principles predict about user behavior in this situation."

Part 3 — Propose a forward path. End with an action, not an argument. "I'd suggest we run an A/B test — version A with your approach, version B with mine, and let the conversion data decide." Or: "I'm happy to explore your direction. Can you give me 2 days to wireframe both options so we can compare them side-by-side?" These endings move the conversation from "who wins the argument" to "what do we do next to get better information."

"That's worth exploring — the intention makes sense. The risk with [X] is [Y] based on [evidence or principle]. I'd suggest we try [Z] and measure [metric] to see which direction works better."

The Most Dangerous Pushback Response — Pure Agreement

When a PM says "can you just make it more colorful" or "add more banners" or "the CEO wants this feature by next week," the worst response is immediate agreement without pushback. Not because the PM is always wrong — but because your job is not to be an execution machine. It is to be a thinking partner.

When you agree to everything, you become a production resource. You lose the strategic positioning that justifies your salary level. And you often end up shipping things that hurt the product — because the PM's instinct was right about the goal but wrong about the solution, and you needed to translate goal into better solution.

The opposite failure — refusing to compromise and digging in on every decision — damages trust and working relationships. The skill is knowing when to push back (when you have evidence or principle supporting your position) and when to defer (when the stakeholder has contextual knowledge you do not have, or when the decision is genuinely close enough that either direction is acceptable).

A useful internal heuristic: "Will I be comfortable defending this decision in a review meeting 6 months from now if it negatively affects the metrics?" If yes, proceed with deference. If not, push back now.

Research as Pushback — Principles to Cite in Meetings

One of the most powerful tools for pushing back on bad design decisions without conflict is citing well-established research. You are not saying "I know better." You are saying "here is what behavioral science consistently observes about how humans make decisions."

The paradox of choice: when users are presented with too many options, they frequently choose nothing. More options do not always lead to more conversions. Often they lead to fewer. When a PM asks to "add more features to the homepage," this principle is your evidence.

Serial position effect: users remember items at the beginning and end of a list much better than items in the middle. When asked to "just add it to the list," this principle supports a conversation about position.

Cognitive load theory: the human working memory can hold approximately 4 chunks of information simultaneously. Design that requires users to hold more than this in mind while completing a task creates errors and abandonment. When asked to add more fields to a form "for data collection," this principle supports a conversation about form length.

Miller's Law: items are more processable when grouped into meaningful chunks. This supports grid layouts, grouped navigation, and section headers over single long lists.

The fear of pushback is usually not about being wrong — it is about conflict. Most people avoid conflict even when they know their position is right. ❌ Wrong Direction ▼

Either capitulating immediately ("sure, I'll make it more colorful") or digging in defensively ("that's not good UX"). Both extremes cost you credibility. Capitulation makes you a production resource. Defensiveness makes you difficult to work with. The formula is the middle path.

Practice is the only thing that makes it good.

The Verbal Case Study

Why This is the Actual Hiring Decision

Every other asset in your portfolio supports the verbal case study. The long-form case study builds credibility. The video creates curiosity. The micro posts establish expertise. The presentation deck frames the conversation. But the moment a hiring manager says "tell me about your work" — what comes out of your mouth in the next 30 seconds determines whether you get the job.

Not because the rest does not matter. Because everything builds to this. The hiring decision is made in conversation — in the moments where someone is deciding whether they trust you, whether they can imagine you presenting your work confidently to their team, whether they believe you are the kind of designer who will improve their product.

This happens verbally. It cannot be delegated to a document. It cannot be replaced by a prototype. It is you, your clarity, your confidence, and your story.

The Hook-First Principle

Most designers describe their work in the wrong order. They start with context and arrive at the interesting part too late.

"So I was working on an ESOP grant management platform at my previous company. The company was called Tallect, they do HR tech, and the platform was used by HR teams at enterprise companies to manage equity compensation for employees. The project started when we noticed that..."

By the time you get to the interesting part, you have lost the listener's active attention.

The correct order: start with the most compelling thing. Then provide the context that makes it even more compelling.

"I designed a grant management system that cut enterprise ESOP processing from 23 days to 10 — not by building something new, but by redesigning how 47 different employee actions were sequenced and validated. Here's what made it interesting..."

Now the listener is in. They want to hear more. The context you provide next will land because they are engaged, not because they are politely waiting to ask a question.

The Three Versions — Know All Three

You should have three practiced, fluent versions of your verbal case study for every project in your portfolio.

60-second version (phone screen / elevator pitch): This version contains: one hook sentence, one business context sentence, one decision with its reasoning, one outcome. The listener should understand what you worked on, why it was hard, and what you achieved — in one minute.

5-minute version (first interview / casual portfolio review): This version adds: 2–3 decision stories (not all of them — the most interesting ones), a brief description of the research or insight that drove your direction, and an honest reflection on what you would do differently. The listener should understand how you think about design problems.

15–20 minute version (portfolio round / deep-dive interview): This version covers the full arc: business context and metric, user journey and emotional stakes, research methodology and key insights, 4–5 design decisions with trade-offs, testing and iteration, outcome and measurement, retrospective. The listener should feel they understand the project as deeply as you do.

Practice all three out loud, repeatedly, without notes. The measure of readiness is when the 60-second version sounds natural, not rehearsed — when the words come automatically because you have said them enough times that they belong to you.

🛠️ Practice Session 4 hours

Build and rehearse 3 versions of your ESOP verbal case study:

  • Version 1 — 60 seconds: Hook + one decision + one outcome. Practice this until it is automatic. This is your phone screen answer.
  • Version 2 — 5 minutes: Hook + user context + 2 key decisions with rationale + outcome + what you'd improve. This is your "tell me about a project" answer in a first round.
  • Version 3 — 15 minutes: Full story — business context, user journey, 4 key design decisions with trade-offs, research insights, what you tried and abandoned, what shipped, how you'd measure it, what the next version looks like. This is your portfolio round presentation.

For each version: write the full script, then practice reading it aloud, then practice without notes. Record the 5-minute version. Listen back. Note where you lose energy or confidence — those are the moments you need to rebuild with better storytelling.

💭 Reflection 30 min

Most people speak about their work in the wrong order. They start with "so we had this product" and describe the context for 3 minutes before anything interesting happens. The hook needs to come first. "I designed a grant management system that reduced processing time from 23 days to 10 days at enterprise scale — here's how I got there." That one sentence is more powerful than 2 minutes of context-setting.

Getting Hired Without Applying — The Inbound Strategy

Why Inbound is Worth More than Outbound

When you send 100 cold applications, approximately 5 recruiters will look at your portfolio. That is a 5% visibility rate. When a recruiter finds you through LinkedIn, through a post, through a referral — they will look at your portfolio with 100% probability. They approached you. They are interested before they have seen anything.

This is not just a mathematical advantage. It is a psychological one. A recruiter who finds you has already formed a positive impression. The conversation starts from a position of interest rather than indifference. The interview feels different. The probability of an offer is significantly higher.

How LinkedIn Actually Works as a Lead Generation Tool

LinkedIn distributes content based on events — any action you take on the platform is data that LinkedIn uses to understand your interests and expertise, and to determine who else should see your content and activity.

When you like posts, LinkedIn notes your interests. When you comment on posts, LinkedIn sees you as engaged with that topic and surfaces your comments to others. When you post original content, LinkedIn initially shows it to a small audience. If that audience engages, the distribution expands. The more specific and useful your content is to a specific audience, the more that audience engages, and the wider the distribution grows.

Practical implication: posting one highly specific, insightful observation about UX design, B2B product design, or HR tech will reach more relevant people than posting a generic "here is my portfolio" announcement. The specific post attracts exactly the people who care about what you know.

The 7-Touchpoint Follow-Up Rule

Most people send one follow-up message when they don't hear back and conclude they are being ignored. They are not. They are simply forgotten. Professionals are overwhelmed with messages, meetings, and tasks. Not responding does not mean not interested.

Follow up 7 times before concluding a lead is dead. Each follow-up should be brief, add a new piece of value (a new post you wrote, a relevant insight from your recent project work, a question about something interesting they said publicly), and never express frustration. "Saw your post about conversion optimization in SaaS products — thought you might find this micro case study from my grant management project interesting" is a follow-up. "Just checking in again" is noise.

The 7-touchpoint rule is not about persistence for its own sake. It is about staying in someone's awareness long enough that when they have a role to fill, you are the first person they think of.

Mock Interviews and Final Integration

What Interviewers Are Actually Evaluating

In a portfolio round or UX interview, your interviewer is simultaneously evaluating several different things. Understanding what they are evaluating helps you know what to prioritize in your preparation.

They are evaluating your thinking process — not just what you decided, but how you thought about the decision. Can you articulate a design problem clearly? Can you name the trade-offs in your approach? Do you show awareness of your own assumptions?

They are evaluating your business awareness — not just whether you know what a conversion rate is, but whether you instinctively connect your design decisions to business outcomes. Designers who speak in metrics without being asked are worth significantly more than designers who can only explain their metrics when asked directly.

They are evaluating your confidence under pressure — not arrogance, but the ability to hold your position when challenged with a question, propose an alternative when your first answer is shown to be incomplete, and acknowledge uncertainty without losing composure.

They are evaluating your self-awareness — can you honestly say what you would have done differently? Can you name a decision you regretted and explain what you learned? Designers who present their work as perfect are less trustworthy than designers who present it as their best thinking at the time, with honest reflection on its limitations.

The Questions That Always Come Up

In nearly every design interview at a quality product company, some version of these questions will appear:

"Walk me through a project you're proud of." — This is the verbal case study. Start with the hook. Lead with the business problem and metric. Walk through your 3 most important decisions. End with outcome and reflection.

"What would you have done differently?" — Do not say "nothing, I'm happy with it." Think honestly about a decision that, with hindsight, could have been better.

"How did you validate this decision?" — If you have data: cite it. If you have research: reference it. If you have neither: say "at the time, I validated it through [what you actually did — heuristic review, competitor analysis, internal review]. In retrospect, I would have wanted to run a quick guerrilla test to validate the [specific assumption] before committing to high-fidelity work."

"Tell me about a time you disagreed with a stakeholder." — Use the pushback formula. Name the situation, explain the reasoning, describe the process (not a conflict — a structured disagreement), and share the outcome.

"What is one thing that's broken in [a product they use]?" — This is a live audit test. Apply Layer 1 immediately. Name one specific observation. Connect it to a metric. Propose one specific change with brief rationale.

The Scrutiny Phase

By now you have read the interviews and hot seat reviews. The pattern is consistent: Anudeep's tone shifts the moment a student begins explaining a decision with real reasoning. The scrutiny phase is where you either confirm or destroy the positive impression created in the scan.

The scrutiny phase questions are designed to test one thing: do you understand your own work? Not "can you describe it" but "do you understand why it works, where it might fail, and what you would do differently?"

The mindset for scrutiny: every question is an opportunity to show how you think, not just what you did. "Why did you use a table view?" is not a gotcha — it is an invitation to walk through your reasoning. Answer it with the decision story structure.

The Mindset for the Final Integration

By Day 30, you will not have mastered everything in this guide. No 30-day program produces mastery. What you will have is a foundation that is ready to accelerate through real application.

The skills that compound fastest with real work experience: business language (every sprint meeting is practice), stakeholder communication (every design review is practice), lean research (every feature is an opportunity to test with one real user), and root cause thinking (every PM brief is an opportunity to ask one more "why").

The skills that require continued deliberate practice outside of work: emotion mapping (practice this on every product you use), decision story writing (document your decisions in real time, not just at case study writing time), and the verbal case study (practice this weekly, even when you are not job hunting — it will improve every meeting you are in).
