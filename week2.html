Week 2: Problem Finding & The Audit

Build the ability to look at any product and immediately see what is broken, why it is broken, and what the real problem is — not the surface symptom. The biggest mistake you will unlearn this week is the tendency to treat the first problem you see as the real problem. The surface problem is what users say. The root problem is why they are saying it. Your job is to find the root.

Day 8 The 3-Layer UX Audit
SHORT ▾
💡The Big Idea

A UX audit is not a checklist. It is a structured way of seeing what your eyes would naturally miss. The 3-layer framework gives you a repeatable approach: gut scan, heuristic pass, data pass. Each layer reveals a different class of problem.

📖Study Session 2 hours

The 3-Layer UX Audit

What a UX Audit Actually Is

A UX audit is not a heuristic checklist. It is a structured way of identifying where users are experiencing friction, confusion, or lost opportunity — and then connecting those observations to business impact. Every audit finding should be answerable with: "this affects [specific metric] because [specific user behavior]." If you cannot complete that sentence, the finding is incomplete.

Layer 1 — The Gut Scan

Before any framework, before any heuristics, before any data — use the product as a first-time user. Navigate without instructions. Write down every moment you feel anything. The gut scan questions are brutally simple:

  • What is this screen trying to get me to do?
  • Is it obvious? How many seconds did it take me to figure it out?
  • What was the first thing my eye went to?
  • Did I hit any moment of hesitation, even small?
  • What would make me leave this page right now?

Write these reactions immediately, without editing. The gut scan captures what a first-time user feels — and first impressions are permanent. The mistake many designers make: they try to be objective and end up being analytical. The gut scan is supposed to be subjective. Your gut reaction is data. Trust it.

Layer 2 — The Heuristic Pass

Go through the product screen by screen, checking against these 5 core heuristics — the ones that surface the most critical issues in real products:

1. Clarity of Purpose
Does every screen have one clear primary action? Multiple competing priorities cause drop-off — users cannot determine what to do and often do nothing.
2. Feedback & System Status
When a user does something, does the product communicate that something is happening? Missing feedback creates anxiety. Users who don't know if their action worked will click again (errors) or leave (abandonment).
3. Error Prevention & Recovery
Are there moments where a user could easily make a mistake? When errors occur, is recovery clear? "Invalid input" is worse than "Please enter a 10-digit phone number without spaces."
4. Consistency
Do similar things look and behave the same way throughout? Inconsistency forces users to re-learn the interface repeatedly, increasing cognitive load on every interaction.
5. Cognitive Load
How much is the user being asked to process, remember, or decide at once? Too many options on a single screen. Too many form fields. Dense paragraph text where scannable bullets would serve better. Every additional cognitive demand is friction.

Layer 3 — The Data Pass

Validate heuristic observations with actual behavioral data. If you have access:

  • Heatmaps — where users actually click vs. where you expect them to click. Reveals false affordances and missed affordances.
  • Session recordings — the path a real user took. Where they paused. Where they scrolled up and down repeatedly (confusion). Where they rage-clicked (frustration).
  • Funnel analytics — exactly where users are dropping off. A 40% drop-off at step 3 is a design failure that needs a design solution.

If you don't have access to data, your Layer 3 is hypothetical: "To validate this finding, I would look at the exit rate on this screen." This shows interviewers you understand the data dimension even without access.

Priority Framework — Sorting Your Findings

After completing all three layers, you likely have 10–30 issues. Prioritize using impact vs. effort:

  • High impact, low effort: Do immediately. Button label change, moving CTA above the fold, adding success messages.
  • High impact, high effort: Plan for next sprint. Checkout flow redesign, navigation overhaul.
  • Low impact, low effort: Do when there is time. Minor inconsistencies, visual alignments.
  • Low impact, high effort: Deprioritize entirely.
🛠️Practice Session 3 hours

Do a full 3-layer audit on one product you use daily.

Layer 1 (30 min)

Use the product as a first-time user. Write raw reactions. What would make you leave right now?

Layer 2 (60 min)

Go screen by screen through one core flow. Check each of the 5 heuristics. For each issue: what is it, where is it, why does it matter, severity (H/M/L)?

Layer 3 (30 min)

State what data you'd want to validate each Layer 2 finding. "To validate this, I'd look at ___."

📝Daily Assignment SHORT 1–3 hours

Write a 5-finding audit summary in the format a senior designer would share with a PM. For each finding, write:

  1. What is the problem (specific, not generic)
  2. Where it occurs in the flow (screen name or step number)
  3. Why it likely matters for the business (which metric it affects)
  4. One specific recommendation to fix it
  5. Priority: High / Medium / Low (with your reasoning)

The format should be readable in under 5 minutes. No fluff. No academic language.

💭Reflection 30 minutes

Which layer was hardest? Most designers find Layer 1 (gut scan) harder than expected — not because it requires knowledge but because they overthink it. The gut scan is supposed to be messy and immediate. If you were writing carefully and slowly during Layer 1, you were in your head, not in the user's shoes.

Auto-saved ✓
✅ Right Direction▾

An audit that connects every finding to a business metric and ends with prioritized, actionable recommendations.

❌ Wrong Direction▾

An audit that lists 20 problems without prioritizing them or connecting them to impact. A 5-finding audit with clear recommendations is worth 10 times more than a 50-finding audit with no clear action.

Day 9 Root Cause Thinking — The Senior Designer's Superpower
SHORT ▾
💡The Big Idea

Every design problem has a surface symptom and a root cause. Surface: "the button is hard to find." Root: "users don't expect to find this feature in this part of their journey." If you fix the surface symptom without addressing the root cause, the problem will come back in a different form.

📖Study Session 2 hours

Root Cause Thinking

The Surface vs. The Root

Every design problem has two layers. The surface layer is what you observe — the symptom. The root layer is why it exists — the cause. Designing at the surface level produces solutions that fix the symptom but leave the cause intact. The problem recurs, often in a different form.

Example: surface observation: "Users are not using the bulk actions feature in the data table." Surface-level design response: "Make the bulk actions button bigger." This might increase click rate. But if the root cause is that users don't trust the feature with important data, increasing button prominence will not fix the actual problem.

The 5 Whys — The Most Important Diagnostic Tool

State the problem. Ask "why does this problem exist?" Get an answer. Ask "why does THAT exist?" Repeat 3–5 times. By the 4th or 5th "why," you are usually close to the root cause.

Example — ESOP Grant Acceptance:
Problem: Users are dropping off at Step 3 of the grant acceptance flow.
Why? Because the step asks them to upload a signed physical document.
Why? Because the compliance team requires a wet signature.
Why? Because the current legal framework treats this as an official contract.
Why? Because the legal team has not validated whether e-signatures are legally equivalent.
Root cause: The UX solution is not to redesign the upload screen — it is to work with the legal team to explore e-signature solutions. The design response is completely different because you found the actual root.

The "Pause and Check" Habit

At every major milestone in a project (after research, after wireframing, before high-fidelity), write one sentence: "The core problem I am designing for is ___."

Then check: is that still the right problem? Has your research revealed a different root than you started with? Does the solution you are designing actually address that core problem, or has it drifted?

A Live Example — The Salony Nykaa Case

A student designed a feature to improve the discoverability of Nykaa's skincare quiz. Initial approach: place the quiz higher in the homepage navigation. But research revealed something different: users were not going to the homepage to find a quiz. They were searching for specific products and feeling overwhelmed by the options. They were leaving the app to ask friends on Instagram because they didn't know what product was right for their skin type.

Surface problem: "quiz is hard to find." Root cause: "users feel uncertain about product choice while actively shopping, and the quiz could help them decide — but only if it appears at the moment of uncertainty, not at a passive discovery moment."

The correct design response: surface the quiz at the product listing page, when users are looking at 200 moisturizers and feeling overwhelmed. Same feature, completely different placement, completely different effectiveness.

The Danger of Solving at the Wrong Level

"Users feel anxious about making mistakes in the grant table, so I added a confirmation dialog before every action."

The root: users do not trust themselves with complex data operations because they do not have enough visibility into what the action will do before they commit.

Surface fix: "are you sure?" dialog. Adds friction to every action, even simple ones.

Root-level fix: show a preview of consequences before the user commits. "This will update the vesting schedule for 47 employees. Here is a summary of the changes." Now the user has actual information. Confidence eliminates anxiety.

🛠️Practice Session 3 hours

Take 3 design problems you have personally faced in your HR tech work. For each one, do a "5 Whys" analysis. Write this out in full for all 3 problems. Then write: was the solution you shipped addressing the surface or the root? If surface — what would a root-level solution have looked like?

📝Daily Assignment SHORT 1–3 hours

A PM sends you this feedback: "Our users are not using the bulk actions feature in the grant management table. They're doing everything one by one."

  1. What is the surface observation?
  2. Ask "why" 3 times. What are the possible root causes?
  3. Which root cause would you prioritize investigating first and why?
  4. What would you design differently if the root cause is:
    • "users don't know it exists" vs.
    • "users don't trust it will work correctly" vs.
    • "users are worried about making a mistake at scale"

Same feature. Three different root causes. Three completely different design directions. This is why root cause matters.

💭Reflection 30 minutes

Think about a decision you made in a past project that you later realized was fixing the wrong problem. Write about it honestly. What did you miss? What would you do differently?

Auto-saved ✓
✅ Right Direction▾

Always asking "but WHY is this a problem?" at least 3 times before deciding what to design.

❌ Wrong Direction▾

Redesigning something because "it looks old" or "the PM said so" without understanding why it is underperforming. This leads to solutions that are technically complete but experientially meaningless.

Day 10 Idea Generation — Emotion-First Thinking
MEDIUM ▾
💡The Big Idea

The best ideas in UX do not come from brainstorming sessions. They come from a deep understanding of what the user is feeling at a specific moment. When you map an emotion correctly, the solution suggests itself.

📖Study Session 2 hours

Idea Generation — Emotion as the Source of Ideas

The Myth of Brainstorming

Most designers believe ideas come from "brainstorming" — a group exercise of generating random possibilities. This produces a lot of ideas quickly. It also produces ideas that have no connection to actual user need. The better model: ideas come from understanding the specific emotional state of a specific user at a specific moment in their journey.

The reason this works: every powerful design feature is solving an emotional problem. Uber showing you the driver's name, photo, and car before they arrive doesn't solve a practical problem — it solves an emotional one: the anxiety of waiting for a stranger. The idea came from someone asking "what is the user feeling right now?"

The Emotion-Idea Bridge

For each emotional state in a user journey, there is a category of design response that addresses it:

Uncertainty

Social proof, comparisons, expert guidance, clear success indicators, transparent consequences of actions.

Overwhelm

Progressive disclosure, intelligent defaults, filtering that understands context, curated recommendations, reducing optionality.

Distrust

Transparency, confirmation messages, reversible actions, visible audit trails, showing data provenance.

Frustration

Better error states, contextual help, escape hatches, alternative paths, anticipatory design that prevents the error.

Delight

Celebration moments, progress indicators, personalized responses, reducing the effort required for the next success.

The Ola Retention Insight

A student designed a business cab booking feature for Ola to improve retention. She had built functional features: fair fare breakup, punctuality indicators, GST invoices. All useful. None particularly sticky.

The insight that changed everything: professionals who use Ola for business travel have predictable, repeatable routes. They are not making fresh decisions every time — they are executing a routine. The emotional state: mild frustration at being asked to re-enter the same information every time for a trip done 20 times. And underlying that: a desire to feel like the product knows them.

The idea: a "saved business trips" system. When a user books "Samsung office visit" for the first time, all details are stored. Booking that trip again is a single tap. Ola knows the route, the driver preference, the timing, the payment method. Retention is addressed not through a notification or loyalty program — but through a single design insight that came from understanding the user's emotional relationship with a repetitive task.

How to Generate More Ideas in Less Time

  1. Write the emotion with specificity. Not "frustrated" but "frustrated that she has to re-enter the same 7 fields for a trip she has booked 15 times this year and she is about to be late for a meeting."
  2. Ask: "What would make this feeling completely go away?" Write 3 answers without filtering.
  3. Ask: "What would make this feeling not just go away but actually flip into a positive emotion?" Write 2 answers.
  4. Look at your 5 answers. One or two will stand out as genuinely interesting and implementable. These are your candidates for development.
🛠️Practice Session 3 hours

Take the LinkedIn connection/messaging flow. Map 6 distinct user emotions across the flow. For each emotion, generate 2 ideas that directly respond to that emotion — not general "improvements" but specific responses to the specific feeling. After generating 12 ideas, identify your top 3 most likely to move a business metric.

📝Daily Assignment MEDIUM 4–6 hours

Pick one of these problem statements:

Option A: Improve the onboarding experience of Keka (or any HR SaaS) for new employees on their first day.

Option B: Reduce the number of support tickets raised by managers in the ESOP platform.

Option C: Increase the frequency of performance review submissions in a 360 feedback tool.

  1. Identify the user journey for this scenario
  2. Map 6 emotions across the journey
  3. Generate 2 ideas per emotion (12 total)
  4. Converge to your top 3 ideas
  5. For each top idea: what emotion does it address, what metric does it affect, and what would you need to validate it?
💭Reflection 30 minutes

Notice which emotion was hardest to generate ideas for. Usually it is the ambivalent emotions — neither strongly positive nor strongly negative. These are the richest opportunities in any product. Users in ambivalent states are the most receptive to a well-designed nudge.

Auto-saved ✓
✅ Right Direction▾

Asking "what is the user feeling at this moment?" before asking "what should the design do?"

❌ Wrong Direction▾

Running a "brainstorming session" that generates random feature ideas without anchoring them to a specific user emotion in a specific moment. Random ideas produce random outcomes.

Day 11 Divergent and Convergent Thinking in Practice
SHORT ▾
💡The Big Idea

Divergent thinking = generate as many options as possible, ignoring all constraints. Convergent thinking = select the best option within real constraints. Senior designers can switch between these modes on demand. Most juniors stay in one mode and wonder why their work either feels uncreative or impossible to ship.

📖Study Session 2 hours

Divergent and Convergent Thinking Applied

The Real Skill — Switching Modes on Demand

Most designers default to one mode and stay there. Execution-focused designers stay in convergent mode: they receive a brief, generate one or two ideas, and execute the most reasonable one. They produce work quickly but miss the genuinely innovative solution because they never explored the full solution space.

Research-focused designers stay in divergent mode: they explore endlessly, generate dozens of ideas, and struggle to commit to a direction. Excellent designers can do both, separately, on purpose.

Rules for Pure Divergent Thinking

When you are in divergent mode, you must follow one rule: no judgment. No evaluation. No "but we can't build that." Every idea that occurs to you gets written down.

Practical technique: set a timer for 15–20 minutes. Write down every idea that comes to mind. Number them. Keep going until the timer ends. The rule is one idea per minute minimum. If you slow down, write the most ridiculous, unimplementable idea you can think of — this often breaks the block and generates several real ideas immediately after.

After the timer ends, do not evaluate. Just count. You should have at minimum 10–15 ideas. If you have 20+, your divergent mode is working.

Rules for Pure Convergent Thinking

After divergent generation, convergent thinking requires you to add all constraints back. The convergent pass questions:

  • Which 3 of these ideas most directly address the root problem (not the surface symptom)?
  • Which 3 are most implementable within the actual time and engineering constraints?
  • Which 3 are most likely to move the specific metric this project is trying to affect?
  • Of the ideas that score well on all three: which one do you have the strongest design rationale for?

The best idea is not necessarily the most creative one. It is the one you can defend most clearly in terms of user benefit, business impact, and technical feasibility.

Why Wireframing is a Divergent Tool

Wireframing is supposed to be a low-stakes, rapid exploration of structural and architectural options. It is divergent design work. But when designers make wireframes that look like black-and-white versions of their final UI — with real spacing, real components, real visual hierarchy — they have moved into convergent execution mode.

Real wireframes are boxes, labels, and arrows. They communicate structure, not presentation. They can be drawn in 5 minutes per screen. The moment a sketch takes "too long to change," you have stopped diverging and started executing. Catch this moment and pull yourself back.

🛠️Practice Session 3 hours

Brief: "Add a way for grant admins to communicate with individual employees about their ESOP status."

Phase 1 — Divergent (45 min)

Generate 12 ideas. Literally any idea. In-app chat? Push notifications? Email digest? AI-generated status summaries? Scheduled video call integration? Slack integration? A physical letter template generator? Write everything. No filtering.

Phase 2 — Converge (45 min)

Now add real constraints: 1 developer, 2 weeks, B2B SaaS with enterprise clients requiring strict data security. Which 2 of your 12 ideas survive? Why?

Phase 3 — Rationale (30 min)

For the 2 ideas you selected, write the full design rationale formula. Include why you rejected the other 10 ideas explicitly.

📝Daily Assignment SHORT 1–3 hours

A PM tells you: "We want to make it easier for HR managers to export ESOP data for their quarterly board presentations."

  1. Generate 8 ideas (divergent — ignore constraints)
  2. Apply constraints: engineering team has 5 days, product already has basic CSV export, users are non-technical HR professionals
  3. Converge to 1 idea
  4. Write the full rationale
💭Reflection 30 minutes

Were you more comfortable in divergent or convergent mode? Most designers who come from agency backgrounds tend to over-converge. Most from academic UX backgrounds tend to over-diverge. Know which bias you have.

Auto-saved ✓
✅ Right Direction▾

Treating divergent and convergent as two separate phases that you consciously switch between, never mixing them.

❌ Wrong Direction▾

Judging ideas while generating them (killing divergent mode), or generating too many ideas without ever committing to one (avoiding convergent mode). Both are ways of avoiding the actual hard part of design work.

Day 12 Wireframing as a Thinking Tool
SHORT ▾
💡The Big Idea

Wireframes are NOT deliverables. They are thinking on paper. A wireframe that looks like a polished black-and-white version of your final UI is not a wireframe — it is a lie. True wireframing is low-fidelity because your clarity is still low.

📖Study Session 2 hours

Wireframing as Thinking (Not as Deliverable)

The Fundamental Misunderstanding

The most common junior mistake in portfolio building is treating wireframes as a deliverable to show stakeholders or interviewers. This misunderstanding leads to wireframes that are polished, structured, and completely defeat the purpose of wireframing.

Wireframes are thinking tools. They are how you get ideas out of your head and into a form where they can be examined, discussed, and changed. A wireframe that will be replaced by a real design in 3 days has already fulfilled its complete purpose if it helped you make a better design decision.

The Fidelity Principle Applied to Wireframing

Recall the fidelity principle: fidelity is about clarity, not visual quality. A wireframe is low-fidelity because your clarity is low at the point where you make it.

The trap: jumping straight to Figma with real UI components creates false fidelity. Your output looks high-clarity, but your actual clarity is still low. This makes you emotionally attached to decisions you have not yet validated. When someone challenges those decisions, it feels like a personal attack on your finished work.

Start rough. Get clear. Then get polished.

What Wireframing Reveals That High-Fidelity Work Hides

When you force yourself to work in low fidelity, you discover things you would miss in a polished UI:

  • Whether the information hierarchy makes sense structurally, before any visual styling
  • Whether the number of actions on a screen is appropriate or overwhelming
  • Whether the flow from screen to screen is logical or creates confusion
  • Whether the core feature is genuinely valuable when stripped of visual presentation
  • Where the architectural trade-offs are — decisions about information grouping, navigation, and relationships between sections

A polished UI can make a bad structural decision look acceptable. A rough wireframe exposes it immediately. This is why wireframing before high-fidelity work catches expensive problems while they are still cheap to fix.

🛠️Practice Session 3 hours

Take 2 emotion-first ideas from Day 10. For each idea:

Round 1 — 15 min per idea

Sketch 3 completely different structural approaches. No real components. No styling. Just boxes and labels. The goal is 3 genuinely different architectural decisions, not 3 slightly different visual treatments.

Round 2 — 30 min per idea

Choose one approach from Round 1. Add one more layer of clarity — slightly more specific layout, which key information appears where, what the primary action is. Still no real UI.

Round 3 — Reflection

Write one paragraph per idea: what did the sketching process reveal that you didn't know before you started?

📝Daily Assignment SHORT 1–3 hours

Take the Zepto daily essentials feature architectural problem: when two kits both contain the same item (e.g., both morning kit and evening kit have milk), what happens in the cart?

Sketch 3 different architectural approaches:

  1. Approach A: Kits are separate cart entities — items in one kit don't merge with items in another
  2. Approach B: Kits merge into a single cart — duplicate items are consolidated automatically
  3. Approach C: User is prompted when a conflict is detected and asked to choose

For each approach, write one sentence: what does this approach make easier for the user, and what does it make harder?

💭Reflection 30 minutes

Did you find yourself wanting to skip to high-fidelity Figma work? That pull is the design equivalent of wanting to start typing code before you know what you are building. The pull is real. The discipline is in resisting it.

Auto-saved ✓
✅ Right Direction▾

Using the roughest possible representation of an idea at its earliest stage, and only adding fidelity as your clarity increases.

❌ Wrong Direction▾

Making "wireframes" that are black-and-white versions of your final UI. This is called cargo-cult wireframing. It looks like process but produces no additional clarity. Interviewers immediately recognize it.

Day 13 Information Architecture — Story First, Always
MEDIUM ▾
💡The Big Idea

Information architecture cannot be represented in a sitemap. IA is the architecture of experience that emerges naturally when you think story-first. If you can tell the story of the experience, the IA will reveal itself. If you draw the IA first, you will get lost in mathematical combinations of roads and never find the actual experience.

📖Study Session 2 hours

Story-First Information Architecture

Why Sitemaps Are the Wrong Starting Point

When you think in sitemaps first, you end up designing roads instead of journeys. You design the network of paths without thinking about the experience of traveling on them. You produce a mathematically complete map with no emotional life.

The deeper problem: sitemaps make you design for all possible users and all possible journeys simultaneously. This produces a product that is "everything to everyone" and nothing in particular to anyone. The best products are designed for specific users having specific experiences — and the architecture emerges from those specific journeys, not the other way around.

The Story-First Method

Before you think about architecture, tell the story. One user. One goal. One journey. Told in narrative form, with emotions, with decisions, with context.

The story should include: who the user is and what they want, the moment they open the product and what they see, what they decide to do first, what happens when they do it, what they feel at each step, what they decide next, and where the story ends.

This story forces you to make every architectural decision that matters. By the time you finish telling it, you have already decided:

  • What the user sees first (what information has highest priority?)
  • What navigation exists (how does the user move between different areas?)
  • What the core flow is (what is the primary path through the product?)
  • What features are visible vs. hidden (what does a user encounter naturally vs. discover later?)

The sitemap is the documentation of these decisions, not the source of them.

The Movie Principle

Think of your product story like a movie. A good movie tells one story — one protagonist, one core journey, one arc. It does not simultaneously tell the story of every character who might interact with the protagonist. When Anudeep asks students to tell the story of their product "like a movie," he is enforcing this discipline: Tell one arc. Follow one user. Go from beginning to end in a way that builds.

The product does not become simpler by telling one story — it becomes more navigable. When you have designed one core journey with clarity and care, the auxiliary journeys (edge cases, secondary users, less common paths) are much easier to add because you have a clear structural backbone to extend.

What Story-First Thinking Surfaces

The most important thing story-first thinking surfaces: the moments of trust. In every product journey, there are critical moments where the user either trusts the product and continues — or doubts the product and leaves. These moments are only visible when you tell the story with emotions.

"The user opens the app and sees their progress dashboard. They feel a flash of uncertainty — is this data accurate? It shows them 3 incomplete tasks, but they thought they finished one yesterday." That uncertainty is an architectural problem: where does the data come from, and how does the product communicate its provenance? A sitemap would not reveal this. A story does.
🛠️Practice Session 3 hours

Take a product idea from your assignment bank or a new HR tech concept. Tell its core story in 500 words. Rules:

  • One user. One specific goal. One journey.
  • Write it in first person (as if you are the user)
  • Include the moment of discovery, confusion, trust, and completion
  • Do NOT write about screens — write about what the user sees, feels, and decides

After writing the story, extract the IA decisions it forced you to make. You should find at minimum 5 architectural decisions embedded in the story.

📝Daily Assignment MEDIUM 4–6 hours

PM Brief: "We want to build a feature where managers can track their team's ESOP vesting progress across the year and send updates to team members."

  1. Write the full user story (300 words, story format, one manager, one scenario)
  2. Extract the 5 IA decisions the story forces you to make
  3. Write which of those decisions is the hardest one (the one with the most trade-offs) and why
  4. Sketch the one screen that is the heart of this feature — the screen where the manager's most important action happens
💭Reflection 30 minutes

Where in the story did you find yourself wanting to jump to a technical solution? That jump is the point where your product thinking stops and your execution mode starts. The quality of your designs depends on staying in story mode longer before jumping to execution mode.

Auto-saved ✓
✅ Right Direction▾

Starting every new product design challenge with a story of one user going through one scenario. Letting the structure emerge from the story.

❌ Wrong Direction▾

Starting with a sitemap or navigation structure before you have a story. This produces technically complete but experientially empty products. The architecture is correct, but nobody wants to use it.

Day 14 Week 2 Integration Day
HIGH ▾
💡The Big Idea

Everything from this week — the audit, root cause thinking, emotion-first ideation, divergent/convergent modes, wireframing, story-first IA — all of it leads to one thing: designing the right thing, not just designing the thing right.

📖Study Session 2 hours

Integration — The Complete Design Cycle

The Loop

By the end of Week 2, you have a complete toolkit for finding and framing the right design problem. The loop looks like this:

Layer 1 audit reveals gut-level friction → Layer 2 audit identifies structural and heuristic problems → Root cause analysis moves you from surface symptom to actual cause → Emotion mapping reveals the specific emotional state driving the problem and the opportunities alongside it → Divergent ideation generates multiple possible responses → Convergent selection commits to the most defensible option → Wireframing tests the structural logic → Story-first IA ensures the architecture serves a real human journey.

This is not a linear process you follow in order. It is a loop you can enter at any point, depending on what the project needs. On a 1-day sprint, you might do a 15-minute Layer 1 audit, a quick root cause check, one round of divergent ideation, converge immediately, and wireframe in 30 minutes. On a 3-month project, every stage gets a full cycle.

The quality of your design output is determined not by the time you spend in each stage but by the quality of thinking you bring to each stage.

No New Study Material Today

Re-read your audit from Day 8, root cause analysis from Day 9, ideation work from Days 10–11, and wireframes from Day 12. Look for the thread that connects them: audit found problems, root cause identified the real problem, ideation generated solutions, wireframes tested the best solution. This is a design cycle — a loop you can enter at any point.

📝Daily Assignment HIGH Full day

Full product audit + design cycle on one real product. Pick one of: CRED's bill payment flow, Zepto's onboarding, or Keka's employee dashboard.

Deliver all of the following:

  1. Layer 1 audit: 5 raw gut reactions (bullet format, no editing)
  2. Layer 2 audit: 3 heuristic findings with severity and metric impact
  3. Root cause: For your most severe finding, write the 3-why analysis
  4. Emotion map: Map 4 emotions across the flow (2 gaps, 2 opportunities)
  5. Ideas: 3 divergent ideas for the most important opportunity
  6. Recommendation: 1 converged recommendation with full rationale formula
  7. Success metric: 1 metric you would track to measure the impact of your recommendation

This is the format of a real design audit deliverable at a product company.
